EdTech Evidence Checker

Is this product likely to help your students? An honest assessment based on what 126+ studies actually show about when education technology works.

Why this tool exists: U.S. schools spend $26–41 billion per year on edtech. Only 4% of educators use evidence when making purchasing decisions. Of the top 100 products, fewer than a quarter have evidence of positive impact. This tool applies what the research actually shows about when technology helps learning.

About the product you're evaluating

1
Evidence Quality

Has anyone actually tested whether this product improves learning? The quality of evidence matters more than its existence.

What is the strongest independent evidence that this product improves student outcomes?
Look for studies NOT funded by the vendor. Check OECD, J-PAL, What Works Clearinghouse, or ESSA evidence databases.
Why this matters: Of 11,000+ edtech products on the U.S. market, fewer than 10% of AI-powered tools have been independently validated. (BU EVAL Collaborative)
Does the evidence measure actual learning, or just engagement?
Time-on-platform, login frequency, and "engagement scores" are not learning. Look for test scores, skill mastery, or grade improvement.
The engagement trap: Engagement metrics can increase while learning stays flat or declines. The ECLS-K study found kindergartners using edtech "almost every day" scored lower in both reading and math. (ECLS-K:2011, 13,000+ children)
2
Teacher Role

The single strongest predictor of whether edtech works: does it amplify what good teachers do, or try to replace them?

How does this product position itself relative to teachers?
Read the marketing carefully. "Personalized learning without teacher intervention" is a red flag. "Helps teachers identify struggling students" is a green one.
The evidence is clear: Brookings, MIT, and the OECD all converge — edtech works when it amplifies what good teachers already do. It fails when positioned as a substitute. (MIT J-PAL, 126 studies)
Does implementation include teacher training and support?
The product itself is only part of the equation. Without training, even good tools underperform.
The training gap: In humanitarian AI deployments, 64% of workers reported receiving no training — and only 38% believed the tools led to better decisions despite 93% adoption. The pattern holds in education. (Humanitarian AI Barometer, 2025)
3
Scope & Specificity

Constrained tools that solve specific problems outperform comprehensive platforms. Simple beats ambitious.

How specific is the learning problem this product addresses?
A tool for "math fact fluency in grades 2-4" is more likely to work than a tool for "personalized learning across all subjects."
Specificity works: Stanford found technology was most effective for teaching decoding — "a fairly constrained construct." For complex skills like reading comprehension, even personalization, gamification, and interactive feedback showed no effect. (Stanford GSE)
Does the product try to do too many things?
Feature count is inversely correlated with evidence quality. Products that do one thing well are more likely to have been tested for that thing.
4
Equity Impact

EdTech was supposed to close gaps. The evidence shows it usually widens them. Check whether this product works for your most vulnerable students.

Has this product been tested with students similar to yours — including your most disadvantaged students?
A product tested in suburban districts with high-speed internet may not work in rural or Title I schools. Look for evidence from comparable populations.
The equity inversion: High-achieving students consistently extract more value from edtech. Low-achieving students often see no benefit or negative effects. The ECLS-K study found gap-widening effects that increased with grade level. (ECLS-K:2011)
What does this product require beyond the product itself?
Requirements like reliable broadband, quiet study space, literate parents, or 1:1 devices disproportionately exclude the students who need help most.
The access reality: 43% of U.S. adults earning under $30,000 lack broadband. Even where device gaps have closed, a "digital use divide" persists — access alone hasn't led to meaningful improvements in teaching and learning. (2025 connectivity data)
5
Cost Reality

The right comparison isn't "this product vs. nothing." It's "this product vs. the best alternative use of the same money."

What is the total per-student annual cost, including infrastructure, training, and IT support?
License fees are often the smallest part. Include devices, bandwidth, IT staff time, teacher training, and ongoing support.
Have you compared this to the best non-technology alternative for the same budget?
Text messages to parents ($1-2/student/year) often outperform platforms costing 100x more. Tutoring, smaller class sizes, and teacher professional development all have strong evidence bases.
The cost comparison: Among the most cost-effective interventions in J-PAL's 126-study review were simple text messages to parents. Utah's evidence-based math tools showed students using tested products were 57% more likely to test proficient — but only 9 of hundreds of products passed evaluation. (MIT J-PAL, Utah STEM Action Center)
6
Your Measurement Plan

Even if the product has evidence, does your implementation plan include knowing whether it's actually working for your students?

How will you know if this product is improving learning in your context?
The vendor's dashboard is not your measurement plan. What will you compare against?
Do you have a plan to stop using it if it doesn't work?
Sunk cost pressure is real. Contracts, training investment, and curriculum integration make it hard to walk away — which is why you need an exit plan before you start.
