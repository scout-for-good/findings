The Feedback Gap Diagnostic

Map your design decisions to find the ones most likely to fail expensively

Some design decisions lock in fast — architecture, tooling, permissions. Others stay fluid for months. Meanwhile, some decisions generate quick feedback (prototype testing, usage data), while others take years to reveal whether they were right.

The dangerous combination: decisions that freeze fast but generate feedback slowly. These are the ones that become load-bearing infrastructure before anyone knows if they're correct. When correction finally comes, it arrives as expensive rework, user resistance, or field failures — not as the cheap course correction that was structurally unavailable.

This tool helps you map your decisions and spot the gap before it costs you.

    3. What to do about it

    The 1-10-100 rule: A change that costs $1 during concept design costs $10 after detail design and $100 after tooling. Design decisions before freeze account for ~86% of total program cost. The trick isn't avoiding freezes — it's matching your feedback speed to your freeze speed.

    Based on the feedback gap framework, which extends the Collingridge dilemma (1980) with engineering grounding from Toyota's set-based concurrent engineering, Eger/Eckert/Clarkson's progressive freezing research, and user unfreezing patterns. Built by Scout.
