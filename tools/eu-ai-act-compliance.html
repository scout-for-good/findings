Can This System Comply?

An EU AI Act compliance readiness assessment for high-risk AI systems

August 2, 2026 — Annex III high-risk AI system requirements become enforceable. Non-compliance penalties: up to 7% of global annual revenue.

Several tools already help you determine whether the AI Act applies to your system and what risk category it falls into. This tool asks a different question: once you know you need to comply, can your system actually do it?

Some systems can be brought into compliance through documentation, monitoring, and process changes. Others have structural properties — training data with no provenance records, black-box architectures that resist explanation, or organizational dependencies — that make compliance technically or practically impossible without fundamental redesign. Industry surveys suggest over half of organizations lack even a systematic inventory of their AI systems, and only 18% have implemented AI governance frameworks.

This assessment evaluates five dimensions of compliance readiness, each mapped to specific Articles of the AI Act. It won't tell you whether the Act applies — use the EU AI Act Compliance Checker for that. Instead, it identifies where your hardest compliance barriers will be and whether you face a documentation project or a fundamental redesign.

Before you start: This tool assesses one AI system at a time. If you haven't inventoried the AI systems in your organization — including third-party tools, embedded models, and AI-powered features your teams may have adopted independently — that inventory is your actual first step.

Compliance Readiness Assessment

1

Value Traceability

Articles 9, 11, Annex IV

The AI Act requires documented risk management and technical documentation covering design decisions, training data, and testing. This is only possible if the value choices embedded in the system are traceable — if you can identify what was chosen, why, and what alternatives were considered.

Can you identify the key design decisions that shaped how this system behaves — what it optimizes for, what tradeoffs were accepted, what populations it was designed to serve?
Is there documentation of the training data: where it came from, what populations it represents, what was excluded, and how quality was assessed?
Note: Training data provenance that wasn't captured at training time generally cannot be reconstructed after the fact. This is widely cited as the single largest structural blocker to compliance programs.
If a regulator asked "why does your system make this specific decision?", could you answer in terms of identifiable inputs and rules — or only in terms of statistical correlations?
2

Behavioral Monitorability

Articles 9, 12, 72

Article 9 requires "continuous iterative" risk management throughout the system's lifecycle. Article 12 requires automatic event logging. This means ongoing behavioral monitoring — not just logging what happened, but detecting when the system's behavior drifts from its intended values.

Does the system currently log its decisions in a way that allows retrospective analysis of patterns, drift, and anomalies?
Could you detect if the system started producing systematically different outcomes for a specific population — within days, not months?
Does the system's behavior change over time (through retraining, online learning, or changing input distributions) in ways that are not directly controlled?
3

Human Oversight Feasibility

Article 14

Article 14 requires that high-risk AI systems can be "effectively overseen by natural persons." This means humans must be able to understand the system's outputs, intervene when needed, and override or halt the system. Some systems make this structurally impossible — through speed, opacity, or volume.

Does the system make decisions at a speed or volume where meaningful human review of individual decisions is impractical?
Article 14 requires systems designed to be "effectively overseen by natural persons." Research suggests explainability and oversight are far more effective when incorporated during design rather than retrofitted to existing architectures.
When a human reviewer disagrees with the system's decision, can they override it effectively — and do they actually do so?
Do the people overseeing the system have enough context and expertise to recognize when it's wrong?
4

Remediation Capacity

Articles 9, 20, 21

The AI Act requires corrective action when systems don't conform (Article 20) and incident reporting (Article 21). This assumes the system can actually be fixed when problems are found. Some systems resist remediation — every fix introduces new problems, or the system's architecture makes targeted changes impossible.

If monitoring revealed the system was producing biased outcomes for a specific group, could you fix it without degrading performance for other groups?
How long would it take to implement a corrective change, from identifying a problem to deploying a fix?
Has the system ever been significantly modified after deployment? What happened?
5

Organizational Alignment

Articles 16, 26, 27

Compliance isn't just technical — it requires cross-functional coordination, executive commitment, and willingness to accept the costs of change. Article 27 requires a Fundamental Rights Impact Assessment. Article 16 assigns obligations to providers throughout the lifecycle. If the organization's incentives conflict with compliance, technical solutions won't be enough.

Does the organization have executive-level sponsorship for AI governance, with clear accountability and budget?
If compliance required reducing the system's performance or changing its outputs, would the organization accept that tradeoff?
Do the teams needed for compliance — legal, data science, engineering, domain experts, affected community representatives — currently coordinate on this system?

This assessment identifies structural barriers to compliance — it is not legal advice and does not determine whether your system is classified as high-risk under the AI Act. For classification, consult the EU AI Act Compliance Checker by the Future of Life Institute, and seek qualified legal counsel. Note that the technical standards supporting the AI Act are still being developed — the two standardization bodies tasked with producing them are targeting late 2026, and the European Commission's guidance on high-risk classification under Article 6 has been delayed. This means some requirements may be further clarified or refined. However, the structural barriers this tool identifies — missing data provenance, oversight gaps, architectural opacity — will remain relevant regardless of how standards evolve.
