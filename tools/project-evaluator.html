Tech for Good Project Evaluator

An honest structural assessment of whether your project is likely to help — based on evidence, not optimism.

Why structural evaluation? Most tech-for-good projects never measure their impact. Of those that try, perception bias contaminates the results. This tool evaluates your project's structure against five criteria drawn from evidence on what actually works. It's not a guarantee — but it's more honest than vibes.

Based on research from Carnegie Endowment, Harvard Ash Center, OECD, UNDP, J-PAL, and 30+ project evaluations.

Describe Your Project

1
Augmentation vs. Replacement

Does your project amplify what people already do, or require them to adopt entirely new behaviors?

The evidence: M-Pesa augmented existing money-transfer behavior — 70% adoption. One Laptop Per Child tried to replace educational systems — no measurable improvement. Every documented success in tech for good follows the augmentation pattern.
What are your users doing today without your tool?
If the answer is "nothing" — that's a warning sign. It usually means you don't understand the problem yet.
How much does your user's daily routine change?
Adoption friction kills more projects than bad technology.
2
Infrastructure vs. Application

Is this something others can build on, or a standalone solution to a specific problem?

The evidence: DHIS2 serves 3.2 billion people across 100+ countries. KoboToolbox is used by every major UN agency. These are infrastructure. Standalone civic tech apps have an extremely high mortality rate — most are abandoned within 2-3 years.
Can other projects build on yours?
Infrastructure compounds. Applications don't.
What happens when you stop maintaining it?
Solo-maintained projects die when the maintainer moves on. What's the sustainability plan?
3
Feedback Loops

Can users see the effect of their participation? Can you tell if it's working?

The evidence: Taiwan's vTaiwan achieves 80% government responsiveness. Cambridge's FindIt reached 50% of the city. These work because they close the loop. Open data portals without action mechanisms show minimal impact — transparency without accountability.
Can your users see what happened as a result of using your tool?
If there's no path from "user does something" to "user sees what happened," engagement won't sustain.
How will you know if your project is actually helping?
Most tech-for-good projects track outputs (downloads, users) not outcomes (did anything change?).
4
Cost Accounting

What does this cost — and who pays? Costs are more measurable than benefits.

The evidence: When you can't measure whether something helps, you can still measure what it costs. Cost accounting is resistant to the effort illusion — you can feel productive while being slower, but you can't feel like you're not paying a hosting bill.
Who bears the costs of your project beyond you?
Think broadly: infrastructure providers, data subjects, maintainers of dependencies, displaced workers, the environment.
Compared to the best non-technology alternative for the same budget, is your project a better use of resources?
The relevant comparison isn't "my project vs. nothing" — it's "my project vs. the best alternative for the same time and money."
5
Reversibility

If this doesn't work, can you stop? Or does adoption create dependencies that survive the tool?

The evidence: AI coding tools became unmeasurable not because of technical limits, but because developers built habits and identities around them. 30-50% of developers in one study refused to work without AI even temporarily. When adoption forecloses evaluation, you've committed before you know if it works.
If your project turns out not to help, how hard is it to remove?
Favor interventions where you can run a time-limited pilot and walk away cleanly.
Can you run a meaningful pilot before full commitment?
The best projects can be tested small before they're deployed wide.
