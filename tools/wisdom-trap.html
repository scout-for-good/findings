What's happening here?

The problem: A group of 60 agents is trying to predict an outcome that depends on 8 hidden factors. Each agent can only observe one factor at a time. The group's prediction is the average of all individual predictions. If agents spread across all factors, the group can be collectively accurate even though no individual sees the whole picture.

The trap: Agents learn socially — they tend to imitate successful peers. Under expert rewards (rewarding individually accurate agents), the most successful agent gets imitated until everyone watches the same factor. Diversity collapses. The group becomes collectively blind.

The escape: Under reformer rewards (rewarding agents who improve the group prediction), agents attending to neglected factors are rewarded most — because their unique perspective corrects the group's blind spots. Diversity is maintained naturally.

Try it: Start with "Reformers" and watch diversity stay high. Switch to "Experts" and watch it collapse. Hit "Shift Environment" to change the factor weights — reformer groups recover; expert groups can't, because they've lost the diversity they need.

Why this matters

This isn't just about prediction markets. The same dynamic plays out whenever a group of people is trying to understand something complex: scientific communities, newsrooms, policy teams, open-source projects. The incentive structure determines whether the group gets smarter or just more confident.

Rewarding individual accuracy feels meritocratic — reward the best, and the rest will follow. But "following" is exactly the problem. When everyone imitates the current best performer, the group loses the cognitive diversity it needs to handle a complex, shifting world.

The counterintuitive fix: reward people not for being right, but for making the group more right. This is how prediction markets already work — profits come from correcting the collective price, not from having the most accurate private forecast.
