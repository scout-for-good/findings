Replacement Design Workshop

Design concrete replacements for frozen values — because diagnosis alone doesn't produce change.

Change succeeds when there's a specific, validated replacement ready to implement. Change fails when the replacement is described only in the negative ("stop doing X") or in aspirations ("be more fair").

This workshop walks you through designing a concrete replacement for a value choice that's been frozen into a technical system. It draws on what actually worked — like the NYC CERCA coalition, which changed algorithms in 7 of 9 health systems by having a specific replacement equation ready to deploy.

You'll work through seven phases:

  1. 1Identify the frozen value — what choice is embedded in the system?
  2. 2Map its function — what does this value actually do? What depends on it?
  3. 3Diagnose the harm — who is affected, and how?
  4. 4Classify the freezing — is this good ice that should stay, or bad ice that needs replacing?
  5. 5Specify replacement requirements — what must the replacement do and not do?
  6. 6Design the replacement — describe the concrete alternative
  7. 7Assess readiness — how close is this replacement to being implementable?
What this tool does: It structures your thinking and surfaces gaps. It cannot design the replacement for you — that requires domain expertise. What it can do is make sure you've asked the right questions and haven't skipped the steps where most replacement efforts stall.
Phase 1 of 7
Identify the Frozen Value
A frozen value is a value choice that's been encoded into a technical system and become invisible, automatic, and hard to change.
Name the specific system, algorithm, tool, or process.
Describe the specific decision or assumption that's been embedded. Not the system — the choice within it.
If you know the history, describe it. If not, say so — unknown origins are themselves a diagnostic signal.
Phase 2 of 7
Map Its Function
Before you can replace something, you need to understand what it's doing. Frozen values persist because they serve a function — even if it's not a good one.
What job is it doing? Why was it put there? Be specific — "efficiency" is too vague.
Other systems, processes, decisions, or workflows that rely on the current behavior.
Not just "the system" — which specific actors, roles, or institutions gain from this staying frozen?
Why this matters: If you can't articulate what the frozen value does, you can't design something that does the job differently. Most failed replacements fail because they address the harm without preserving the legitimate function.
Phase 3 of 7
Diagnose the Harm
Be specific about what this frozen value does to whom. Vague harm descriptions produce vague replacements.
Identify specific groups or populations. "Everyone" is almost never the right answer.
Trace the causal chain from the frozen value to the harm. What specifically happens?
How many people are affected? Over what time period?
Phase 4 of 7
Classify the Freezing
Not all freezing is bad. Constitutional rights, safety interlocks, and professional standards are frozen values too — deliberately chosen to protect people. Score the current frozen value on five markers to determine whether it's good ice (keep it) or bad ice (replace it).
Phase 5 of 7
Specify Replacement Requirements
A replacement must do two things: preserve the legitimate function and eliminate the harm. This phase helps you specify what "better" actually means.
What legitimate functions must it preserve? Be specific — each requirement should be testable.
What specific harms must it avoid? What assumptions must it not make?
Technical, institutional, regulatory, cost, or timeline constraints that any replacement must respect.
What would you measure? What outcome would prove the replacement is better than what it replaced?
Common failure: Requirements that are too vague to test. "More fair" isn't a requirement. "Reduces the racial disparity in referral rates from X% to below Y%" is. If you can't specify how you'd know it's working, the replacement isn't ready.
Phase 6 of 7
Design the Replacement
This is the hard part. Not "what's wrong" but "what instead." A concrete replacement is something specific enough that someone could implement it on a specific date.
What specifically replaces the current frozen value? Be as concrete as you can — a named method, a specific process, a defined algorithm, a particular policy.
Has it been tested? Published? Deployed elsewhere? If no evidence exists yet, say so — that's a readiness signal.
What's the implementation path? Who needs to act? What's the sequence of steps?
Every replacement introduces its own frozen values. Name them. This is how you ensure the new ice is good ice.
The CERCA test: Could you walk into an organization tomorrow and say "use this instead"? If the answer is "we need more research first," that's honest — but it means you have a research plan, not a replacement. Both are valuable; don't confuse them.
Phase 7 of 7
Assess Replacement Readiness
How close is your replacement to being deployable? These five dimensions separate a ready replacement from a research direction.
