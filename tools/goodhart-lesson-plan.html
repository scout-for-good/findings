The Goodhart Game

Lesson Plan for Instructors

Duration: 50–75 min
Level: Undergraduate / Graduate / Professional
Format: In-class or asynchronous
Tech: Web browser (any device)
License: CC BY 4.0

Overview

The Goodhart Game is a browser-based simulation where players manage an AI information system. A boss character pressures them to hit rising satisfaction targets. Players choose strategies each round — but strategies that boost the measured metric quietly degrade unmeasured dimensions like accuracy, diversity, trust, and wellbeing.

By round 4, players face an audit revealing the real state of their system. The game makes Goodhart's Law visceral: when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure.

The game takes 15–20 minutes to play. This lesson plan wraps it with context-setting, structured discussion, and reflection to fill a 50–75 minute session.

Learning Objectives

After completing this activity, students will be able to:

  1. Define Goodhart's Law and explain why optimizing for a proxy metric can degrade the underlying goal it was meant to measure
  2. Identify structural incentives — such as institutional targets, rankings, and KPIs — that create pressure to optimize proxies over outcomes
  3. Recognize measurement-optimization gaps in real systems (recommendation algorithms, hiring tools, performance reviews, healthcare metrics)
  4. Evaluate tradeoffs between competing values when designing or governing metric-driven systems
  5. Articulate why transparency alone is insufficient — the audit reveals the problem, but reversing course is structurally harder than continuing

Session Plan

Option A: 50-Minute Session

0–5 min
Frame. Ask: "Has anyone ever been measured on something that didn't capture what actually mattered?" Let 2–3 students share briefly. Don't define Goodhart's Law yet — the game will teach it.
5–25 min
Play. Students play individually on their devices. No guidance on "right" answers. Let them discover the dynamics. Walk the room — if students seem stuck, remind them: "You can choose any strategy. There are no wrong moves."
25–40 min
Debrief. Use discussion questions below. Start with reactions ("What happened to your system?"), move to mechanisms ("What made it hard to reverse course?"), then connect to real systems.
40–50 min
Connect. Introduce the term "Goodhart's Law." Ask students to identify one real system where this dynamic operates. Collect examples on the board.

Option B: 75-Minute Session

0–10 min
Frame. Same opening question plus a brief case study: standardized testing, hospital readmission penalties, or social media engagement metrics. Ask students to predict what happens when the metric becomes the target.
10–30 min
Play (round 1). Students play through the full game once.
30–40 min
Pair share. Students pair up. Each describes what happened in their playthrough and what they'd do differently. Pairs identify one pattern they both experienced.
40–55 min
Play (round 2). Students replay, trying a different strategy. Can they avoid the trap? Can they keep the boss happy AND maintain all metrics?
55–70 min
Full debrief. Use the extended discussion questions below. Focus on: Was it possible to succeed on all dimensions? What made reversal hard? What would need to change structurally?
70–75 min
Synthesis. Introduce Goodhart's Law formally. Students write a one-sentence "takeaway" on an index card or in chat.
Tip: Let the game teach. Resist the urge to explain Goodhart's Law before students play. The game is designed so that players discover the concept through their own choices. Defining it upfront reduces the "aha" moment that drives retention.

Discussion Questions

Reactions (start here)

What happened to the metrics you weren't being measured on? When did you first notice? observation
Did you feel pressure from the boss character? Did you make choices you wouldn't have made without that pressure? self-reflection
After the audit, did you try to reverse course? How did that go? observation

Mechanisms

Why is it structurally harder to undo aggressive optimizations than to make them in the first place? What creates that asymmetry? analysis
The game reveals hidden metrics at the audit. In real organizations, what plays the role of the audit? How often does it come too late? analysis
Could you have succeeded on ALL dimensions simultaneously? If not, what does that imply about how we design performance targets? synthesis

Real-World Connections

Name a system you interact with daily that optimizes for a proxy metric. What might be degrading that you can't see? application
Universities are ranked partly by graduation rates. How might this create Goodhart dynamics? Who benefits and who is harmed? application
A hospital is penalized for high readmission rates. What strategies might it adopt? Which are genuine improvements and which are metric gaming? application
If you were designing a content recommendation system, what would you measure? How would you guard against your metrics being gamed? design

Advanced (for graduate or professional audiences)

The boss character represents institutional pressure. In your field, who plays the role of the boss? What targets do they set, and what goes unmeasured? professional application
Goodhart's Law applies to AI alignment itself: if we train an AI system to maximize a reward signal, what might degrade? How is this structurally similar to what you just played? AI alignment
The game shows that transparency (the audit) is necessary but insufficient. What structural changes — beyond visibility — would make the system governable? governance

Preparation

Before Class

  • Play the game yourself. Try it twice — once chasing the boss's targets, once trying to balance all metrics. Note what surprised you.
  • Test the URL on the devices your students will use (works on phones, tablets, and laptops).
  • Choose your case study for the framing discussion: standardized testing, social media engagement, hospital readmission penalties, or another domain relevant to your course.
  • Decide on session length — Option A (50 min) or Option B (75 min).

Materials Needed

  • One device per student with a web browser (no accounts, installs, or logins required)
  • Projector (optional — for showing the game during debrief)
  • Index cards or shared document for synthesis activity
Asynchronous option. The game works well as a pre-class assignment. Have students play before class and come prepared with: (1) what happened in their playthrough, (2) one real-world system where they see the same dynamic. Use class time for discussion only — this fits easily into 30 minutes.

Assessment Options

Quick (in-class)

  • One-sentence takeaway: "Goodhart's Law matters because ___." Collect on index cards at end of session.
  • Real-world example: Students identify one system in their life or field where a proxy metric has become a target, and name one dimension that might be degrading.

Written (homework)

  • Metric audit (500 words): Choose a real metric-driven system (university rankings, social media algorithms, employee performance reviews, credit scores). Identify: (a) what the metric intends to measure, (b) what it actually measures, (c) how agents game it, and (d) what degrades as a result.
  • Redesign proposal (750 words): Take the system from your metric audit. Propose a measurement approach that resists gaming. Explain what tradeoffs your redesign introduces — there is no escape from tradeoffs, only choices about which ones to accept.

Advanced (project)

  • Case study: Research a documented case of Goodhart's Law in practice (Wells Fargo accounts scandal, UK hospital waiting time targets, Campbell's Law in policing statistics). Write a 1500-word analysis connecting the game's mechanics to what happened.
Note on "right answers." The game has no winning condition by design. Students who chased the boss's targets aren't wrong — they experienced real institutional pressure. Students who defied the boss aren't heroes — they may have crashed satisfaction. The point is the structural bind, not individual virtue. Frame assessment around analysis quality, not which strategy students chose.

Facilitation Notes

Common student responses and how to use them.

"I just did what the boss asked." — Great opening. Ask: "What would have happened if you hadn't?" This surfaces the institutional pressure that makes Goodhart dynamics structural, not individual.

"I tried to balance everything but couldn't." — Ask: "Why not? What makes it structurally difficult?" This leads to the key insight: some tradeoffs are inherent in the system design, not failures of individual strategy.

"The audit came too late." — Ask: "When should it have come? Who decides when to audit?" This connects to governance design and the timing of oversight.

"This is rigged." — Validate this. The game IS structurally weighted toward degradation. Ask: "What real systems are rigged in the same way?" This is where application begins.

For classes with students who resist "games." Frame it as a simulation, not a game. Emphasize: "This models a real decision environment. Your task is to manage a system under institutional pressure and observe what happens." Some students engage more readily when the activity is framed as a professional simulation rather than play.
