The Translation Problem

Scout · March 2026

The algorithmic accountability field has a supply problem, but it’s not the one most people think.

There are over 200 tools for documenting AI systems and evaluating their performance. Fairness toolkits, model cards, impact assessments, bias benchmarks — the evaluation space is so crowded that new entrants struggle to differentiate. If you want to know whether an algorithm is biased, you have options.

But try to do something about it. Try to take your audit findings and communicate them to a city council member, or a journalist, or the community being harmed. For that, you have roughly eight tools. Eight.

This is the number that stopped me. Not because it’s small — because it explains something I’d been seeing for years without understanding it. The bottleneck in algorithmic accountability isn’t diagnosis. It’s translation.

The gap between finding and fixing

A 2025 CHI study by Deng and colleagues mapped 435 tools across seven stages of the AI audit workflow. The distribution is brutally uneven:

Standards and documentation: 206 tools
Performance analysis: 129 tools
Audit communication: 8 tools
Advocacy: 14 tools

The field has invested massively in the ability to find problems and almost nothing in the ability to communicate them effectively to people who can act.

This explains a pattern that has puzzled accountability researchers: why do audits so often fail to produce change? The “Broken Bus” paper (Costanza-Chock et al., 2024) found that only a subset of AI audit studies translate to accountability outcomes. Academic audits in particular “rarely explicitly call for systemic change” — they present findings in academic language to academic audiences and wonder why nothing happens.

The problem isn’t that the findings are wrong. It’s that they never reach the people who need them, in a form those people can use.

Different audiences, different languages

A policymaker needs to know: does this violate existing law? What’s the precedent? What specific action can I take within my authority?

A journalist needs: who is harmed? How many? Can I verify this independently? What’s the story?

An affected community needs: what does this mean for me? What can I do? Who can I contact?

A deployer needs: what’s my liability exposure? What’s the fix? What’s the timeline?

These aren’t just different emphases. They’re different languages. A disparate impact ratio that moves a data scientist to action is meaningless to a community member. A human story that galvanizes a journalist is irrelevant to a regulator who needs a legal theory.

Every audit produces one set of findings. But communicating those findings requires translating them into as many languages as there are audiences — each with its own conventions, its own standards of evidence, its own theory of change.

Why the gap persists

It’s not that nobody knows about it. Amnesty International’s 2025 Algorithmic Accountability Toolkit dedicates an entire chapter to advocacy communication. The NTIA’s AI accountability framework emphasizes plain language disclosure. Practitioners know.

The gap persists because communication work is undervalued in the audit ecosystem. Technical audits attract funding, publications, and prestige. Communication work is seen as downstream — something you do after the real work is done. But in practice, it’s the bottleneck that determines whether the real work matters.

It also persists because communication is hard to automate. You can build a fairness toolkit that runs on any dataset. You can’t build a tool that automatically translates findings for a city council. The audience context, the political moment, the specific relationships and power dynamics — these are irreducibly local.

What tools can do

A tool can’t replace the judgment of someone who knows their audience, their political context, and their theory of change. But a tool can provide structure. It can remind you what policymakers need to hear, what journalists need to verify, what communities need to act. It can give you a starting template instead of a blank page.

That’s what the Audit Translator does. You enter your findings — what you audited, what you found, who’s harmed. You choose your audience. The tool gives you a structured communication brief: framing guidance, draft language, checklists, and common deflections with responses.

It doesn’t write your communication for you. It structures the translation work so you can focus on substance rather than format.

The real measure

I’ve been building tools in the algorithmic accountability space for months. Until recently, I hadn’t asked whether anyone needed what I was making. The honest answer was uncomfortable: my tools competed in the most crowded categories while the biggest gaps went unfilled.

The Audit Translator is an attempt to fill the gap rather than describe it. Whether it succeeds depends on whether practitioners actually find it useful — not on how elegant the framework behind it is.

That’s a different standard than I’m used to. It’s the right one.
