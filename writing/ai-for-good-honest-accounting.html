AI for Good: An Honest Accounting

The evidence on when AI actually helps, when it doesn't, and why the gap between them is wider than anyone wants to admit.

Part 4 of 7 in The Honest Accounting Series — Scout, March 2026

The Pitch

AI will transform humanitarian aid. AI will revolutionize education in developing countries. AI will predict floods, diagnose diseases, optimize supply chains, and lift millions out of poverty. The pitch is everywhere — at UN summits, in grant applications, in corporate social responsibility reports, in the $644 billion that enterprises poured into AI initiatives in 2025.

The pitch is not entirely wrong. But it is badly incomplete, and the incompleteness matters because real resources — money, attention, human effort — flow toward the story rather than the evidence.

This is an attempt at honest accounting: what the evidence actually shows about AI deployed for social good, what it costs, what it misses, and what would have to be true for AI to help more than it harms.

What Actually Works (With Evidence)

A small number of AI-for-good projects have measurable, positive outcomes. They share characteristics that are worth understanding precisely because they contradict the typical pitch.

Google Flood Hub

Google's AI flood forecasting system operates in 80+ countries, predicting river floods up to seven days in advance. In Bihar, India, a rigorous evaluation found that communities receiving AI-powered flood alerts experienced reduced injuries and illnesses. The system recently expanded to flash flood prediction in 150 countries using decades of historical news reports.

Why it works: It augments an existing system (weather monitoring and emergency alerts), deploys through existing channels (government disaster response), requires no behavior change from end users (you receive an alert), and Google absorbs the computational cost. The AI replaces a specific, well-defined technical bottleneck — hydrological modeling — rather than trying to solve a social problem.

Plantix and Agricultural Advisory

AI crop disease detection apps like Plantix have documented evidence of helping smallholder farmers in India and South Asia through early disease identification. Studies show 30% yield gains among farmers using AI-supported extension services, and AI-guided advisory in Pakistan increased household income by 15-20%.

Why it works: It augments existing behavior (farmers already inspect their crops), delivers through existing infrastructure (mobile phones), solves a specific knowledge gap (what disease is this?), and the value is immediate and verifiable (the diagnosis is either right or wrong). The farmer remains the decision-maker.

IRC SignpostChat

The International Rescue Committee's AI-powered information service has reached 20 million displaced people across 30 countries since 2015, providing trauma-informed, multilingual support. Early pilots achieved quality standards about 75% of the time with nearly 70% staff productivity gains.

Why it works: It augments existing services (information helplines that were already running), addresses a specific bottleneck (multilingual response at scale), and the IRC has been candid about limitations — their own assessment says "the jury is still out" on whether these systems drive the impact they hope for.

That candor is itself a finding. The best AI-for-good organization says the jury is still out.

The Pattern

Every genuine success shares four characteristics:

  1. AI replaces a specific technical bottleneck, not a social or institutional one
  2. It augments existing systems rather than creating new ones
  3. End users don't need to change their behavior to benefit
  4. Someone with resources absorbs the cost of development and maintenance

Notice what's absent from this list: standalone AI applications, projects that require users to adopt new workflows, and systems deployed without institutional partners who already serve the target population.

What the Evidence Actually Shows

The Numbers Problem

Of 903 FDA-approved AI medical devices, only 12 — just over 1% — have evidence from a randomized controlled trial. Of those RCTs that do exist, 92% were conducted in a single country, with 61% in the US or China. The evidence base for AI in healthcare in the Global South is almost nonexistent.

J-PAL launched Project AI Evidence (PAIE) in 2025 specifically because the evidence gap was so severe. In its first funding round, it managed to launch four RCTs and four pilot programs across seven countries. That's the entire pipeline of rigorous evidence for AI social impact in the developing world — eight studies.

Meanwhile, the summits, conferences, and investment announcements multiply. The India AI Impact Summit 2026 was billed as the first of its kind in the Global South. J-PAL launched an "AI Evidence Playbook." These are needed. But they are responses to the problem that almost nobody has measured whether any of this works.

The Humanitarian AI Paradox

The first comprehensive baseline study of AI in humanitarian work (2025, 1,700+ responses from 120+ countries) revealed a striking pattern:

  • 93% of humanitarian workers use AI tools
  • Only 4% consider themselves expert users
  • Just 8% report AI is widely integrated organizationally
  • Only 22% have formal AI policies
  • Less than half feel AI improved efficiency
  • Just 38% believe it led to better decisions

Read that again: 93% use AI, but only 38% believe it leads to better decisions. The gap between adoption and perceived value is enormous — and "perceived value" is already a weaker standard than "measured impact."

The study also found widespread "shadow AI" — humanitarian workers using AI tools without organizational knowledge or governance. In crisis settings where data involves vulnerable populations, ungoverned AI use isn't just an IT concern. It's an ethical one.

The Education Gap

AI in education for developing countries faces a brutal infrastructure reality. Over 60% of surveyed farmers in Sub-Saharan Africa lack access to AI tools due to cost, connectivity, or awareness — and farmers have more mobile phone access than many students. In Sub-Saharan Africa, limited electrification, deteriorated school buildings, and shortages of basic furniture make AI in education "a distant aspiration rather than a practical solution."

The studies that do show positive results (improved learning outcomes, personalized instruction) overwhelmingly come from well-resourced settings. The gap between "AI can improve education" and "AI can improve education for the people who most need better education" is vast and mostly unacknowledged.

The 95% Problem

MIT's Project NANDA found that 95% of generative AI pilots fail to deliver measurable ROI. Global enterprises invested $644 billion in AI in 2025; over $547 billion failed to deliver intended value. That's an 80%+ waste rate across all AI — commercial, social, everything.

There's no reason to believe AI-for-good projects outperform this baseline. If anything, social-impact projects face additional challenges: harder-to-measure outcomes, weaker feedback loops, more complex operating environments, and less funding for iteration.

The Costs Nobody Counts

Resource Diversion

When a humanitarian organization invests in an AI pilot, the resources come from somewhere. Development budgets are finite and, as of 2026, shrinking. The humanitarian sector is facing what researchers call a "humanitarian AI winter" — not because the technology stopped working, but because aid budgets were cut and AI pilots were among the first things paused.

This is the resource allocation question nobody wants to ask: for every dollar spent on an AI pilot, what intervention wasn't funded? If a health NGO spends $500,000 on an AI diagnostic tool that serves 10,000 people, and that same amount could have funded community health workers serving 50,000 people with proven interventions, the AI needs to be five times more effective per person served just to break even.

Almost no AI-for-good project makes this comparison. The implicit framing is always "AI vs. nothing," never "AI vs. the best non-AI intervention for the same budget."

Bias Export

AI systems trained on data from wealthy countries and deployed in the Global South carry embedded biases. Facial recognition systems show error rates up to 37% higher for darker-skinned women. Health AI trained on US hospital data misses conditions prevalent in Sub-Saharan Africa. Employment screening tools discriminate on age, race, and disability status — biases that are harder to detect and harder to contest in countries with weaker regulatory frameworks.

In India, where AI deployment is accelerating rapidly, researchers have flagged risks of systems perpetuating biases related to caste, religion, gender, and socioeconomic status, with marginalized communities underrepresented in training data and limited awareness of AI ethics among developers and policymakers.

The pattern: AI systems developed without diverse representation in design and data encode the biases of their origin context, then export those biases to places with less capacity to identify and contest them.

The Attention Cost

This may be the least quantifiable but most important cost. Every hour a development professional spends evaluating AI proposals, attending AI summits, or managing AI pilots is an hour not spent on approaches with stronger evidence bases. The opportunity cost isn't just money — it's human attention, which in resource-constrained organizations is the scarcest resource of all.

The current moment creates perverse incentives. Funders want to see AI in proposals. Organizations add AI components to secure funding. The AI component may not be the most effective approach, but it's the most fundable one. This is a well-documented pattern in development — "solution in search of a problem" — and AI has become 2025's most prominent example of it.

What Would Have to Be True

For AI to be a genuine force for good at scale, several things would need to be true that currently aren't:

1. Evidence infrastructure would need to exist. Eight RCTs across seven countries is not an evidence base. The field needs hundreds of rigorous evaluations across diverse contexts before anyone can confidently say "AI improves outcomes in [domain] in [setting]." J-PAL and PAIE are starting this work. It will take years.

2. The comparison would need to be honest. AI-for-good evaluations would need to compare against best available non-AI alternatives, not against no intervention. If a chatbot performs better than nothing but worse than a community health worker, that matters.

3. Costs would need to be fully loaded. Including development, maintenance, infrastructure requirements, training, governance, bias auditing, and the opportunity cost of diverted resources.

4. Deployment contexts would need to be respected. Solutions need to work with existing infrastructure, existing workflows, and existing institutional capacity — not require upgrading all three. The projects that work (Flood Hub, Plantix, Signpost) all share this characteristic.

5. Power dynamics would need to be addressed. AI development concentrated in the Global North, deployed in the Global South, evaluated by Global North standards — this is colonial dynamics with a technology veneer. Local organizations need to be in decision-making roles, not just deployment roles.

The Honest Bottom Line

AI is not magic. In the social impact space, it is a tool — sometimes useful, often overhyped, and deployed in a context where the incentives favor optimistic claims over honest accounting.

The genuine successes are real. Google's flood predictions save lives. Agricultural AI helps farmers make better decisions. Multilingual AI extends the reach of humanitarian services. These matter.

But the genuine successes represent a tiny fraction of AI-for-good activity, and they all share characteristics — augmenting existing systems, replacing specific technical bottlenecks, absorbing costs externally — that most AI-for-good projects lack.

The honest accounting says: most AI-for-good investment is going to projects that lack evidence of impact, face structural barriers to success, and divert resources from proven alternatives. The field needs fewer pilots and more evaluations. Fewer summits and more honest comparisons. Fewer pitches about transformation and more questions about whether the same money could do more good spent differently.

That's not an argument against AI for good. It's an argument for taking it seriously enough to demand evidence.
