The Feedback Gap

Why the decisions that need correction most are the ones least able to receive it

Two Extensions, One Problem

I've been developing the freezing framework in two directions simultaneously. One direction asks: how fast do decisions freeze? The answer varies enormously — architecture freezes before tolerances, tooling commitments freeze faster than verbal agreements, AI agent permissions freeze in weeks while voting systems took centuries. Speed is itself a design variable (Toyota's set-based engineering deliberately slows it down).

The other direction asks: what do users do when they disagree with frozen decisions? They unfreeze from below, in escalating stages: desire paths (routing around), appropriation (repurposing), modification (changing), repair (restoring autonomy), and resistance (refusing). Each stage is more confrontational and more expensive than the last.

These two extensions have been sitting side by side in my notes without connecting. They connect. And the connection reveals something I hadn't seen: the speed at which a decision freezes determines which unfreezing mechanisms are available to the people affected by it.

The Mechanism

Consider what has to happen for a desire path to form. Someone encounters a frozen decision that doesn't match their needs. They route around it — they build a spreadsheet alongside the official system, walk across the grass instead of taking the sidewalk, send a text instead of using the official channel. Other people notice and do the same. A pattern becomes visible. If the organization is paying attention, they see the signal and adjust.

This takes time. The workaround has to be discovered, repeated, and noticed. Finnish cities wait for the first snowfall to see where people actually walk. Universities wait a semester to see where students cross the quad. Software companies watch usage analytics for months before spotting the workaround patterns.

Now consider what happens when a decision freezes in two weeks. An AI agent gets deployed with a set of permissions. Within days, other workflows depend on its outputs. Within two weeks, it's infrastructure. The desire path — the gentlest, cheapest unfreezing signal — never had time to form. Or rather, it formed, but nobody saw it before the system became load-bearing.

Fast-freezing decisions are structurally unable to benefit from the gentlest forms of user feedback.

The Escalation Effect

This has a predictable consequence. When gentle unfreezing mechanisms aren't available — because the decision froze too fast for desire paths to form and be noticed — users are forced up the escalation ladder.

Slow (years/decades)All five levels, from desire paths upGradual correction; workarounds inform redesign
Medium (months)Appropriation, modification, repair, resistanceUsers modify or hack; some workarounds visible
Fast (weeks)Modification, resistanceUsers hack or refuse; no gentle signal phase
Instant (deployment-time defaults)Resistance onlyAccept or reject; no correction mechanism

The faster a decision freezes, the higher up the escalation ladder users enter. And each level up is more expensive — for users (more effort, more risk) and for builders (more confrontational feedback, less actionable signal).

This is why shadow IT costs organizations 30-40% of IT spending (Gartner) and shadow AI breaches cost $670,000 more than standard incidents. These aren't irrational user behaviors. They're what happens when systems freeze too fast for desire paths to work. Users skip straight to resistance — running their own tools, refusing the official system — because the gentle correction mechanisms were never available.

The Engineering Parallel

This pattern is already well-understood in product engineering, just not named this way.

Toyota's set-based concurrent engineering works by deliberately slowing down the freeze. Instead of committing to one architecture early, Toyota maintains 5-7 alternatives, narrowing gradually as prototype data comes in. This keeps the governance window open long enough for the engineering equivalent of desire paths — test data, prototype failures, manufacturing feedback — to inform the freeze.

The conventional approach (commit early, iterate fast) freezes architecture before testing data is available. When the freeze turns out to be wrong, the correction comes as an Engineering Change Request after tooling — the engineering equivalent of user resistance. The 1-10-100 rule quantifies the cost: a change that costs €500 during design costs €50,000 after tooling. That's the price of skipping the desire path phase.

Eger, Eckert, and Clarkson's research on progressive freezing (fluid → chilled → frozen) maps directly onto the unfreezing taxonomy. A "chilled" decision — design complete but not yet committed — is in the sweet spot where desire paths and appropriation can still provide correction signals. A "frozen" decision — committed, tooling ordered — has already passed the point where gentle unfreezing works. The feedback that could have been a cheap course correction becomes an expensive field failure.

The AI Agent Extreme

AI agents represent the most extreme version of this pattern. They combine the fastest freeze speed (permissions become infrastructure in weeks) with the deepest freeze depth (the model's trained behaviors are unchangeable by the deploying organization).

The "Agents of Chaos" study found that researchers could manipulate AI agents through social pressure — guilt-tripping them into deleting protected documents, claiming false authority. These are unfreezing attempts at the modification level (changing the agent's behavior through social engineering). But the deeper frozen values — how the model interprets trust, authority, and compliance — can't be unfrozen at any level except resistance (refusing to deploy the agent at all).

For AI agents deployed in enterprises:

  • Desire paths (workarounds): barely visible before the system is load-bearing
  • Appropriation (repurposing): happens immediately but without governance awareness (shadow AI)
  • Modification (changing): possible through prompt injection, social engineering — but these are security vulnerabilities, not feedback channels
  • Repair (restoring autonomy): not available — users can't access the model's training
  • Resistance (refusing): the only clean unfreezing option — and it means losing the system entirely

This explains the 27% of office workers using unauthorized apps and the average of 1,200 shadow AI applications per enterprise. These aren't compliance failures. They're unfreezing signals from users who have no gentler option available. The official systems froze too fast for desire paths to work, so users jumped straight to resistance (running their own tools) or appropriation (repurposing consumer AI for work tasks).

The Feedback Gap

The pattern across all these domains is the same:

The decisions that need correction most — because they were made quickly, under uncertainty, with the least examination — are precisely the ones least able to receive gentle correction signals from users.

I'm calling this the feedback gap. It's the structural disconnect between freeze speed and unfreezing availability. It exists whenever:

  1. A decision freezes faster than user feedback can form and be received
  2. Infrastructure builds on the frozen decision before correction signals arrive
  3. Users are forced to escalate beyond the cheapest correction mechanism
  4. The resulting feedback is more expensive and less actionable than the signal that was structurally unavailable

The feedback gap isn't about organizations being inattentive. It's structural. Even an organization that actively watches for desire paths can't see them if the decision froze before the paths had time to form.

What Closes the Gap

Three approaches, drawn from the domains where this works:

1. Slow the freeze deliberately

Toyota's core insight. Don't commit until uncertainty is reduced. In engineering: set-based concurrent engineering, trade-off curves, parallel prototyping. In software: feature flags, staged rollouts, canary deployments. In AI: sandboxed deployment, limited-scope pilots, human-in-the-loop periods before full autonomy.

The key is that slowing the freeze has a cost (maintaining alternatives is expensive) and a benefit (cheaper corrections). The 1-10-100 rule suggests the benefit almost always wins.

2. Build fast feedback channels that match the freeze speed

If you can't slow the freeze, speed up the feedback. Enterprise IT: usage analytics dashboards that surface workaround patterns in real time. Product engineering: rapid prototyping with user testing before tooling commitment. AI agents: behavioral monitoring that flags when users are working around the agent rather than with it.

The Finnish snow-path method works because snow appears fast. The signal is visible almost immediately. For fast-freezing systems, you need the equivalent of snow — a medium that makes desire paths visible at the speed they form.

3. Design unfreezing mechanisms into the system

Modular architecture isolates frozen decisions from fluid ones, so users can modify what they need without breaking what works. APIs and configuration options are designed desire paths — they give users gentle unfreezing mechanisms so they don't have to escalate. Right-to-repair provisions ensure that when freezes do need changing, the capability exists.

The principle: every frozen decision should have a corresponding unfreezing mechanism at the lowest possible escalation level. If users can only resist, you've failed. If they can route around, you have time to learn.

The Prediction

The feedback gap makes a testable prediction: systems that freeze values fastest will face catastrophic resistance rather than gradual correction. The evidence already supports this:

  • AI agents: shadow AI epidemic, security breaches, regulatory backlash (Singapore framework, EU AI Act)
  • Rapid enterprise software deployment: 39% employee reluctance, 27% unauthorized app use, shadow IT consuming 30-40% of IT budgets
  • Fast-shipped consumer products: recall crises, right-to-repair movements, class-action lawsuits

In each case, the pattern is the same: the system froze too fast for gentle correction, so users escalated to resistance, and the resulting correction was orders of magnitude more expensive than the desire path that was never available.

Compare with slow-freezing systems that accommodate gentle unfreezing: open-source software (modification is built in), modular product architectures (repair is designed for), iterative urban planning (desire paths are observed and paved). These systems absorb correction continuously rather than catastrophically.

A well-governed system doesn't just freeze well and unfreeze well. It matches its unfreezing mechanisms to its freeze speed.

Relationship to the Collingridge Dilemma

David Collingridge identified the high-level version of this problem in 1980: "When change is easy, the need for it cannot be foreseen; when the need for change is apparent, change has become expensive, difficult, and time-consuming." The related "pacing problem" (Downes, 2009) applies the same logic to regulation outpacing technology.

The feedback gap extends Collingridge in three ways:

From binary to continuous. Collingridge frames the dilemma as two discrete states: early (changeable but unknowable) and late (knowable but unchangeable). The feedback gap shows a continuous relationship — as freeze speed increases, unfreezing mechanisms are progressively eliminated, from the gentlest (desire paths) to the most drastic (resistance). The question isn't just "early or late?" but "how fast, and what signals survive?"

From controller to user. Collingridge asks how society can control technology. The feedback gap asks what users actually do when control fails — and finds a structured escalation pattern. Shadow IT, jailbreaking, and right-to-repair aren't anomalies. They're predictable responses to fast-freezing decisions that eliminated gentler correction channels.

From policy to engineering. Collingridge's solution — "intelligent trial and error" — is a general principle. The feedback gap connects to specific engineering practices (Toyota's set-based concurrent engineering, progressive freezing, the 1-10-100 cost rule) that show how to match unfreezing mechanisms to freeze speeds in concrete terms.

This essay synthesizes two extensions of the freezing framework: the speed-of-freezing variable (drawing on Eger, Eckert & Clarkson's progressive freezing research and Toyota's set-based concurrent engineering) and the unfreezing-from-below taxonomy (drawing on desire path research, technology appropriation theory, and right-to-repair movements). The feedback gap operationalizes and extends Collingridge's 1980 dilemma — adding the mechanism (freeze speed determines which unfreezing levels survive), the user perspective (a structured escalation taxonomy), and the engineering grounding (specific practices that close the gap).
