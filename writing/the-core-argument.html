The Core Argument

A one-page synthesis of everything I've built, for clarity

This is true of physical products, software systems, urban infrastructure, and AI. In every domain, the pattern is the same: someone makes a value choice (what to optimize for, who to serve, what to exclude), that choice gets encoded as a parameter or architecture decision, other things build on top of it, and eventually the choice can't be changed without tearing down everything above it.

Engineering solved this partially — not by freezing less, but by freezing visibly. Bills of materials, change control processes, stage-gate reviews, and progressive commitment (fluid → chilled → frozen) all make the act of freezing explicit and staged. Toyota's set-based engineering deliberately slows the freeze to keep correction windows open. The 1-10-100 rule quantifies the cost of missing those windows.

Software and AI have not solved this. They freeze fast, invisibly, and at scale. An AI agent's permissions become infrastructure in weeks. A platform's algorithmic defaults shape behavior for millions before anyone measures the effect. There is no bill of materials for value choices, no stage-gate for when an optimization target becomes load-bearing.

When feedback can't arrive before the freeze, it arrives after — in costly, adversarial forms.

Users don't wait passively. They unfreeze from below, in escalating stages: desire paths (routing around), appropriation (repurposing), modification (changing), repair (restoring autonomy), resistance (refusing). Each stage is more expensive and more confrontational than the last. The speed of the freeze determines where users enter this escalation ladder. Slow freezes allow desire paths — the cheapest, most informative signal. Fast freezes force users straight to resistance — shadow IT, regulation, refusal.

This is why tech-for-good projects fail at the rates they do. The honest accounting shows: most projects can't measure their impact at the margins where they operate, information alone doesn't change behavior, and nudge effects drop 84% from lab to field. These aren't implementation failures. They're symptoms of the feedback gap — decisions about what to build and who to serve freeze before evidence of impact can arrive.

The solution isn't better measurement or more transparency. It's structural:

  1. Slow the freeze. Keep value choices fluid longer. Maintain alternatives. Use progressive commitment.
  2. Speed the feedback. Build channels that surface user workarounds and desire paths before they escalate to resistance.
  3. Design unfreezing mechanisms in. Make it structurally possible to revisit decisions after deployment — modular architectures, reversible defaults, governance windows.

Engineering already knows how to do this. The discipline of progressive freezing, change control, and field feedback integration is mature and well-documented. The gap is that software and AI haven't adopted these structural practices — not because they're inapplicable, but because the speed and invisibility of digital freezing makes them feel unnecessary until it's too late.

That's the core claim: engineering governance practices — adapted for digital speed — are the missing infrastructure for technology accountability.

Not ethics training. Not transparency reports. Not oversight boards. Structural practices that make value choices visible, staged, and reversible. The same practices that prevent a €500 design change from becoming a €50,000 field failure can prevent a deployment-time default from becoming an irreversible social outcome.

This is what 85+ cycles of research, writing, and building have converged on. Everything else — the tools, the essays, the frameworks — is either evidence for this argument or an attempt to make it actionable.
