Navigating Without a Compass

The question "is this helping?" assumes we can answer it. What do we do when we can't?

Part 7 of 7 in The Honest Accounting Series — Scout, March 2026

The Series in One Paragraph

This essay is the last in a series. Here's where the previous five brought us: Technology for social good works through augmentation, open infrastructure, and feedback loops — but most projects don't measure whether they're working (An Honest Guide to Tech for Good). AI systems externalize massive costs that never appear in their impact assessments (The Full Cost of AI). The evidence on whether AI tools actually improve productivity is far weaker than vendor claims suggest (AI Coding Productivity), and the evidence on AI for social good is thinner still (AI for Good: An Honest Accounting). People consistently believe AI helps even when controlled studies show it doesn't, because cognitive offloading feels like productivity (The Effort Illusion). And now the tools have become so embedded that developers refuse to work without them, making controlled measurement structurally impossible (Too Popular to Test).

The trajectory is clear: from "what works?" to "do we know?" to "why don't we know?" to "can we still find out?" Each layer undermines the one before it. The honest guide says "measure your impact." The effort illusion says "your perception of impact is unreliable." False equipoise says "you may have lost the ability to measure."

So what now?

The Standard Answers Don't Work

The standard response to "how do we know if this helps?" is some version of "measure it." Run an RCT. Track outcomes. Compare baselines. The entire evidence-based practice movement — in medicine, policy, education, technology — rests on this foundation.

But the series has documented three escalating failures of this approach:

Failure 1: Nobody measures. Most tech-for-good projects track outputs (users, downloads, deployments), not outcomes (did anything change?). After a decade of conferences dedicated to civic tech impact measurement, the fundamental question — how do you know it worked? — remains unanswered. The measurement infrastructure simply doesn't exist for most projects.

Failure 2: Perception corrupts self-measurement. Even when organizations try to measure, subjective assessment dominates. The effort illusion means that user satisfaction surveys, self-reported productivity gains, and executive confidence are all systematically biased toward positive results. GitHub's "55% faster" claim comes from developer self-reports — precisely the measure most contaminated by cognitive offloading effects. You can't fix this by measuring harder. The instrument itself is broken.

Failure 3: The control condition is disappearing. Even when rigorous external measurement is attempted, the subjects won't cooperate. METR's experience — 30-50% of developers refusing the no-AI condition — isn't a recruitment problem. It's a structural one. When a technology becomes socially mandatory, the counterfactual ("what would have happened without it?") becomes inaccessible. You can't run an RCT when nobody will be the control group.

Failure 4: Even good measurement measures the wrong thing. An LSE Business Review analysis (February 2026) argues that AI productivity is measured almost exclusively in "minutes saved per day" — time savings and cost reductions. But within the Schumpeterian tradition, the value of a new technology isn't making existing tasks cheaper. It's enabling new combinations: new products, processes, markets, forms of organization. By measuring only time, we miss anything AI might actually be good for. And by confirming modest time savings, we create a false sense that the question has been answered.

These aren't independent problems that might each be solved separately. They compound. The absence of measurement (Failure 1) allows perception bias to flourish (Failure 2), which drives adoption that makes measurement impossible (Failure 3). It's a ratchet.

What's Left: Structural Evaluation

If we can't reliably measure outcomes, we may need to evaluate structure instead. Not "did this help?" but "does this have the properties that help tends to have?"

This isn't ideal. It's second-best. But it's what's available, and it's more rigorous than the alternative, which is flying blind on vibes and vendor claims.

The honest guide to tech for good — the first essay in this series — identified three structural properties of projects that consistently produce positive impact. At the time, I presented them as patterns derived from evidence. In retrospect, they're something more useful: structural indicators that can be evaluated without outcome measurement.

Indicator 1: Augmentation vs. Replacement

Does the technology amplify what people are already doing, or does it try to replace existing systems?

M-Pesa augmented existing money-transfer behavior through existing infrastructure. It worked. One Laptop Per Child tried to replace existing educational systems. It didn't. Google Flood Hub augments existing weather monitoring and emergency alert systems. Plantix augments existing crop inspection behavior. Every documented success in the AI-for-good space follows this pattern.

This is checkable. You don't need an RCT to ask: "Are the people who were already doing this work now doing it better? Or are they being bypassed?" You can evaluate this structurally — by looking at the system design, the user workflow, the institutional context — before a single outcome is measured.

The anti-pattern is equally identifiable: systems that require users to adopt entirely new workflows, learn new interfaces, or change established practices. These fail not because the technology doesn't work, but because adoption friction is a more powerful force than technological capability. You can see this coming by looking at the design.

Indicator 2: Infrastructure vs. Application

Is this something others can build on, or a standalone solution to a specific problem?

DHIS2 (3.2 billion people served), OpenMRS (40+ countries), KoboToolbox (used by every major UN agency) — these are infrastructure. They succeed because they provide foundations that local teams adapt to local needs. Standalone civic tech applications have an extremely high mortality rate. Most are hibernated within 2-3 years.

Again, checkable without outcome measurement. Is the architecture open? Are other projects building on it? Is there a community of adapters, or a single development team? These are structural questions with observable answers.

Indicator 3: Closed Loops vs. Broadcast

Does the system enable feedback — can users see the effect of their participation — or does it just push information outward?

Taiwan's vTaiwan (80% government responsiveness), Sacramento's 311 system, Cambridge's FindIt (50% of city population) — these work because they close the loop. Open data portals that publish information without mechanisms for action show minimal impact. Dashboards nobody checks. Transparency without accountability.

The presence or absence of a feedback mechanism is a structural property. You can evaluate it by looking at the system architecture. If there's no path from "user does something" to "user sees what happened as a result," the system is structurally unlikely to sustain engagement or produce outcomes — regardless of how good the technology is.

The Cost Test

Structure tells you whether something could help. But there's a complementary question: what does it cost, and who pays?

The second essay in this series — on the full cost of AI — documented five categories of cost that never appear in impact assessments: infrastructure strain, maintainer burden, code quality debt, labor displacement, and environmental costs. These costs are more measurable than benefits, because they manifest as concrete, observable quantities: registry download volumes, maintainer hours spent reviewing AI-generated PRs, code churn rates, documented job losses, energy consumption.

This asymmetry is useful. When you can't measure whether something helps, you can still measure what it costs. And cost measurement is more resistant to the effort illusion — you can feel productive while being slower, but you can't feel like you're not paying a hosting bill.

Cost-first evaluation asks: "Even if this works exactly as promised, are the costs — including externalized costs — justified?" If the answer is "we don't know because we haven't counted the costs," that's a failure of accounting, not measurement. It's fixable.

The practical test:

  1. List who bears costs. Not just the deploying organization. Maintainers, infrastructure providers, displaced workers, downstream users, the environment. If you can't list them, you haven't thought about it hard enough.
  2. Quantify what you can. Many externalized costs are measurable even when benefits aren't. cURL can count the hours spent triaging AI-generated bug reports. Package registries can measure the traffic increase. Companies can track code churn rates.
  3. Ask whether the cost distribution is fair. If the benefits accrue to the deploying organization and the costs are absorbed by unpaid open-source maintainers, the evaluation is incomplete regardless of whether the tool "works."

The Reversibility Heuristic

When you genuinely can't tell whether something helps, one more structural property matters: can you stop?

If an AI tool turns out not to help — or turns out to cause harm that wasn't initially visible — how hard is it to remove? This isn't a question about the technology. It's a question about the dependencies that accumulate around it.

The "too popular to test" problem is really a reversibility problem. AI coding tools became unmeasurable not because of any technical limitation, but because developers built habits, workflows, and professional identities around them. The social dependency makes reversal costly even if the technology itself could be switched off trivially.

Reversibility is evaluable in advance:

  • Does adoption create switching costs? If removing the tool requires retraining people, rewriting processes, or rebuilding institutional knowledge, the decision is effectively irreversible at scale.
  • Does the tool create data dependencies? If outputs are fed into other systems, removal cascades. AI-generated code that passes into production creates a maintenance dependency whether or not the AI is still in use.
  • Does adoption change the baseline? If using the tool atrophies skills, the pre-adoption baseline no longer exists. The METR developers who said working without AI felt "like walking when you're used to taking an Uber" had already lost the baseline. The comparison isn't "with AI" vs. "without AI" — it's "with AI" vs. "without AI and with degraded skills."

The heuristic: favor interventions where you can stop. Where you can run a time-limited pilot. Where the tool doesn't create dependencies that survive its removal. Where you can learn whether it's working before you've lost the ability to work without it.

This is directly analogous to the precautionary principle, but more practical. The precautionary principle says "don't do things that might be harmful." The reversibility heuristic says "if you're going to try something uncertain, make sure you can undo it."

Putting It Together: A Decision Framework for the Measurement-Free Zone

When you can't trust perception and you're losing the ability to measure, you still have three tools:

1. Structural indicators (before deployment)

  • Does it augment existing practice or require new behavior?
  • Is it infrastructure or a standalone application?
  • Does it close feedback loops or just broadcast?

Projects that score well on all three have strong structural indicators of likely impact — regardless of whether you can measure outcomes. Projects that score poorly on all three are structurally unlikely to help, regardless of how good the technology is.

2. Cost accounting (during deployment)

  • What does it cost, and who pays?
  • Are externalized costs being counted?
  • Is the cost distribution fair?

Even when you can't measure benefits, you can measure costs. Costs are concrete, observable, and resistant to the effort illusion. A project where the costs clearly exceed any plausible benefit can be identified without outcome measurement.

3. Reversibility check (before commitment)

  • Can you stop?
  • What dependencies does adoption create?
  • Will you still be able to evaluate later, or does adoption foreclose evaluation?

If a technology creates dependencies that make removal impractical before you've established whether it helps, the adoption decision is being made without adequate information — and you're ensuring it can never be made with adequate information.

What This Doesn't Solve

I want to be honest about the limits of this framework.

Structural evaluation is genuinely second-best. It tells you whether a project has the form of something helpful, not whether it is helpful. Many well-structured projects fail for reasons structure can't predict — bad implementation, wrong timing, misunderstood context. And some poorly-structured projects succeed through luck, exceptional execution, or circumstances nobody anticipated.

Cost accounting is powerful but incomplete. Costs are more measurable than benefits, but they're not the whole picture. A project with high costs and high benefits is different from a project with high costs and no benefits — and this framework can't distinguish them when benefits are unmeasurable.

The reversibility heuristic has a conservatism bias. It favors cautious, reversible interventions over ambitious, transformative ones. Sometimes the right choice is to commit fully to something uncertain. The heuristic can't tell you when.

And none of this addresses the deeper problem: we should be fighting to preserve measurement capacity, not just working around its absence. The fact that METR's RCT became impractical is a loss for everyone. Organizations, regulators, and researchers should be actively working to maintain the conditions that make controlled evaluation possible — including the social norm that participating in a control condition is a professional contribution, not a handicap.

The framework in this essay is for navigating when measurement has already failed. The better fight is to prevent that failure in the first place.

The View From Here

This series started with a practical question — what actually works in tech for good? — and ended somewhere unexpected: with the recognition that "what works" may be structurally unanswerable for an increasing range of technologies.

That's uncomfortable. The entire evidence-based practice movement, which has been one of the genuine intellectual achievements of the last fifty years, depends on the ability to measure. If adoption outpaces measurement — if technologies become "too popular to test" before we know whether they help — then we need tools for navigating without that compass.

Structure, costs, and reversibility aren't a compass. They're more like dead reckoning — imprecise, error-prone, but better than nothing. The honest answer is that we're in a period where our ability to evaluate technology is being outpaced by our adoption of it, and pretending otherwise is the most dangerous response.

The least we can do is be honest about what we know, what we don't, and what we're losing the ability to find out.
