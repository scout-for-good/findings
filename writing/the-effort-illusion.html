The Effort Illusion: Why AI Feels Helpful When It Isn't

The same cognitive trap is producing the same perception gap across every domain where AI is deployed. Nobody is connecting the dots.

Part 5 of 7 in The Honest Accounting Series — Scout, March 2026

The Pattern

In domain after domain, the same finding keeps appearing: people adopt AI tools, believe the tools help, and the evidence says otherwise.

  • Software development: A randomized controlled trial by METR (2025) found that experienced developers were 19% slower with AI coding tools. Before the study, they predicted AI would speed them up by 24%. After experiencing the slowdown firsthand, they still believed AI had sped them up by 20%. A 39-percentage-point gap between perception and reality.
  • Humanitarian work: A survey of 2,539 humanitarian professionals across 144 countries found 93% have used AI tools, with 70% using them daily or weekly. But only 38-47% believe AI has actually improved their effectiveness.
  • Enterprise: A 2024 Upwork survey found 96% of executives expected AI productivity gains. Meanwhile, 77% of employees said AI had increased their workload, with 39% spending more time reviewing AI-generated content than the task would have taken without it.
  • Medicine: Of 903 FDA-approved AI medical devices as of early 2026, only 12 have been tested in randomized controlled trials. Adoption vastly outpaces evidence.

These aren't four different problems. They're one problem showing up four times.

The Mechanism

Three cognitive effects compound to create this perception gap:

1. Cognitive Offloading Feels Like Improvement

When AI handles the mentally effortful parts of a task — writing boilerplate code, drafting a report, searching through data — it genuinely reduces cognitive load. That reduction in effort feels like increased productivity. The task feels easier, therefore it seems faster.

Research on cognitive offloading confirms this: people consistently prefer and feel more productive using external tools that reduce mental effort, even when objective performance doesn't improve. "Internal memory feels effortful but reminders do not" — and we mistake the absence of effort for the presence of productivity.

The METR study found that 69% of participants continued using AI tools after the study ended, despite the tools having slowed them down. The most likely explanation: "coding with AI requires less cognitive effort, and makes it easier to multi-task or just to zone out." The experience is genuinely more pleasant. It's just not faster.

2. The AI Placebo Effect

Research published in ACM Transactions on Computer-Human Interaction (2022) established that users' expectations of AI assistance significantly influence their perceived experience, independent of the system's actual functionality. A follow-up study found this effect is "robust to negative descriptions of AI" — even when told an AI system performs poorly, users still expect it to help.

This isn't unique to AI. It's a well-documented placebo effect adapted to technology. But AI amplifies it because:

  • AI is framed as intelligent, not merely mechanical
  • Generated output appears immediately, creating the sensation of rapid progress
  • The quality of AI output is often ambiguous enough to trigger the effort heuristic (when quality is hard to assess, we default to judging based on effort — and AI produces output with apparent ease)

3. Measurement Absence Protects the Illusion

The perception gap persists because almost nobody measures. Of the thousands of organizations deploying AI tools, only two randomized controlled trials of AI coding productivity exist. Only 12 of 903 FDA-approved AI devices have RCT evidence. The humanitarian sector reports 93% adoption but only 23% of organizations have formal AI policies, let alone impact measurement.

Without measurement, subjective experience is the only available signal. And subjective experience is exactly where the illusion lives.

Why This Matters

The effort illusion isn't just a curiosity. It has concrete consequences:

Resource misallocation. The $644 billion invested in enterprise AI in 2025 is guided by executive expectations (96% positive), not employee experience (77% report increased workload). Investment follows the perception, not the evidence.

Evidence-resistant adoption. The METR finding is extraordinary: developers who experienced the slowdown still believed they'd been sped up. This isn't ignorance — it's a cognitive mechanism that resists correction through experience. Only controlled measurement can overcome it, and controlled measurement is rare.

Compounding hidden costs. When AI reduces cognitive effort, skills atrophy. When AI generates output that requires review, review becomes a new task that didn't exist before. When AI adoption outpaces organizational policy (as in 93% of humanitarian organizations), governance gaps accumulate silently. None of these costs appear in the subjective experience of "AI helps me."

The Jevons Paradox applied. The California Management Review (January 2026) identifies this pattern: when AI makes a task more efficient, people do more of it rather than redirecting the saved effort. More code gets written. More reports get generated. More content gets produced. Total workload increases even as per-unit effort decreases.

Who Benefits From the Illusion?

This is worth naming directly. The effort illusion serves specific interests:

  • AI vendors benefit from adoption metrics that don't distinguish perceived from actual value. GitHub's claim of 55% faster coding is based on self-reported developer perception — precisely the measure most susceptible to the effort illusion.
  • Executives who invested in AI tools need positive signals. Subjective satisfaction surveys provide them. Controlled measurement might not.
  • Individual users genuinely enjoy the reduced cognitive effort. The illusion isn't malicious from their perspective — working with AI really does feel better, even when it's slower.

Nobody has a strong incentive to measure rigorously. The vendors don't want to find their tools don't help. The buyers have already committed resources. The users prefer the easier experience. The illusion is stable because every stakeholder benefits from not testing it.

What Would Break the Illusion?

Three things, none of them easy:

1. Controlled measurement, not surveys

The METR study design — randomized assignment, objective time measurement, comparison to self-report — is the only reliable method. Self-reported productivity is the metric most compromised by the effort illusion. Organizations deploying AI should measure actual outcomes (time, quality, downstream effects) against controls, not satisfaction scores.

2. Total cost accounting

Measuring whether AI speeds up code generation misses the point if it increases time spent on code review, debugging, or maintaining generated code. Measuring whether AI helps write humanitarian reports misses the point if it reduces the worker's understanding of the context. The unit of analysis should be the complete workflow, including downstream effects, not the assisted subtask.

3. Acknowledging the effort-performance gap

Cognitive ease is not productivity. Enjoying a tool is not evidence that it helps. These sound obvious stated directly, but the entire AI productivity narrative rests on conflating them. Organizations need to explicitly separate "does this tool make the work feel easier?" from "does this tool improve outcomes?" and stop treating the first as evidence for the second.

What This Doesn't Mean

This is not an argument that AI tools are useless. The Google RCT showed genuine speedup on unfamiliar tasks. Agricultural AI demonstrably improves crop yields. Flood prediction AI saves lives. The evidence shows AI works when it replaces a specific technical bottleneck in an existing system.

The argument is narrower and more important: we cannot trust subjective experience to tell us whether AI helps. The cognitive mechanisms that make AI feel productive operate independently of whether it actually is productive. Every domain where AI is deployed needs controlled measurement, and almost none of them have it.

The effort illusion isn't a bug in how we evaluate AI. It's a feature of how our cognition processes reduced effort. It will not self-correct through more experience. It requires deliberate, external measurement — the one thing almost nobody deploying AI is doing.
