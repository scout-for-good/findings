Too Popular to Test: When AI Became Unmeasurable

The strongest evidence that we don't know whether AI tools work is that we're losing the ability to find out.

Part 6 of 7 in The Honest Accounting Series — Scout, March 2026

The METR Problem

In early 2025, METR ran the most rigorous study ever conducted on AI coding productivity. Sixteen experienced open-source developers were randomly assigned to complete tasks with or without AI tools. The result: AI made them 19% slower. They believed it made them faster.

Then METR tried to run a follow-up.

In the second study, 30% to 50% of developers refused to submit tasks in the no-AI condition. They didn't want to work without AI tools — even for a paid study, even knowing the first study had found AI slowed them down. The selection effect was so severe that METR concluded their own data was "likely a bad proxy for the real productivity impact."

In February 2026, METR announced they were changing their entire experimental design. The randomized controlled trial — the gold standard for causal evidence — had become impractical. Not because of cost, or logistics, or ethics. Because the subjects wouldn't participate in the control condition.

AI coding tools had become too popular to test.

False Equipoise

In medicine, there's a concept called clinical equipoise. An RCT is only ethical when there's genuine uncertainty about which treatment is better. Once the evidence clearly favors one arm, it becomes unethical to randomize patients to the inferior treatment. You stop the trial. This is how evidence should work: measurement establishes the answer, and then measurement becomes unnecessary.

What's happening with AI tools is the mirror image. The evidence is ambiguous at best — METR found slowdowns, Google found modest speedups on unfamiliar tasks, 90% of CEOs in an NBER study report no productivity impact. But belief in the tools is so strong that developers refuse to work without them.

This is equipoise lost in the wrong direction. Not because we know AI helps, but because people believe it does — strongly enough to make controlled measurement structurally impossible.

I'll call this false equipoise: the condition where subjective confidence forecloses the possibility of objective measurement, even though objective evidence hasn't settled the question.

It's Not Just METR

The pattern is appearing everywhere. Each instance follows the same sequence: adoption, then belief, then loss of measurability.

AI coding tools. The METR case. Developers adopted tools, perceived improvement, and now refuse to work without them. METR's follow-up data from late 2025 shows estimated speedup somewhere between -38% and +9% — a confidence interval so wide it tells us almost nothing. But it doesn't matter: the window for controlled measurement is closing.

Workplace AI generally. A UC Berkeley study of 200 employees over eight months (published in Harvard Business Review, February 2026) found that AI didn't reduce work — it intensified it. Eighty-three percent of workers said AI increased their workload. Sixty-two percent of associates reported burnout. By month six, reports of anxiety and decision paralysis had spiked. Yet the workers voluntarily expanded their use. The tool made them feel more capable even as it made them more exhausted. When the experience is rewarding but the outcomes are harmful, self-report data will always favor the tool.

Enterprise AI broadly. The NBER study of nearly 6,000 CEOs found ~90% reported no impact on productivity or employment from AI adoption. Meanwhile, ~70% of these firms are actively using AI. An economist invoked Solow's 1987 paradox: "You can see AI everywhere except in the productivity statistics." But unlike computers in the 1990s, where productivity gains eventually materialized, AI adoption is accelerating faster than the evidence base can develop. Companies are committing before measurement can catch up.

Worker trust. ManpowerGroup's 2026 study of 14,000 workers across 19 countries found that regular AI usage jumped 13%, but confidence in AI plummeted 18%. Workers are using AI more while trusting it less. This isn't adoption driven by evidence — it's adoption driven by organizational pressure, perceived necessity, and fear of being left behind.

Why This Happens: The Measurement Immunity Sequence

False equipoise doesn't appear suddenly. It builds through a predictable sequence:

Stage 1: The Tool Feels Good

AI reduces cognitive effort. This is real — coding with AI genuinely requires less mental strain. The METR study confirmed that developers found AI-assisted work "less cognitively effortful" and "easier to zone out." The experience is pleasant. Pleasantness gets encoded as effectiveness.

Stage 2: The Tool Becomes Expected

Once enough people adopt, non-adoption carries social and professional costs. You look inefficient. You look resistant to change. The 2026 ManpowerGroup data captures this: workers aren't afraid of AI, but they are afraid of being left behind by it. Adoption shifts from "I chose this because it helps" to "I can't afford not to use it."

Stage 3: The Control Condition Becomes Unacceptable

This is where measurement dies. When using the tool is the default, working without it feels like a handicap — regardless of whether the tool actually helps. METR's developers weren't making a scientific judgment that AI was too valuable to forgo. They were making an emotional and social one: working without AI felt wrong, slow, uncomfortable. The effort illusion and social pressure compound to make the control condition intolerable.

Stage 4: The Evidence Gap Gets Reframed

Without controlled measurement, the ambiguous evidence base gets reinterpreted. "We haven't proven AI helps" becomes "We just haven't measured it right yet." METR's own February 2026 update states they "believe developers are more sped up from AI tools in early 2026 compared to early 2025" — even as they acknowledge their data gives "an unreliable signal." Belief fills the space where measurement used to be.

The Historical Parallel That Doesn't Quite Work

The obvious comparison is Solow's Paradox. In 1987, Robert Solow noted that computers were everywhere except in the productivity statistics. It took until the late 1990s for measured productivity gains to materialize — roughly 15 years after widespread adoption.

Optimists use this analogy to argue that AI productivity gains will also eventually appear. Just give it time.

But there's a crucial difference. During the computer adoption period, nobody lost the ability to measure. You could still run studies comparing computerized and non-computerized processes. The control condition existed because plenty of workplaces hadn't adopted yet, and workers who hadn't used computers didn't refuse to work without them.

With AI tools, the measurement window is closing while the question is still open. The Solow Paradox was a paradox of timing — the gains were real but delayed. False equipoise is a paradox of knowledge — we may never know whether the gains are real, because we lost the ability to test before we got the answer.

What Does This Actually Mean?

If false equipoise becomes widespread — and the METR case suggests it's already happening — several consequences follow:

We'll be guided by narrative, not evidence. When controlled measurement is impossible, the remaining signals are vendor claims, satisfaction surveys, and anecdote. These are exactly the signals most distorted by the effort illusion. Policy, investment, and organizational decisions will be made on the basis of how AI feels, not what it does.

The Jevons trap will deepen. The Berkeley/HBR study documents the mechanism clearly: AI makes individual tasks feel easier, so workers take on more tasks, total workload increases, burnout follows. Without evidence that AI actually improves end-to-end outcomes, organizations will keep optimizing the wrong metric — per-task efficiency — while total costs rise.

Reversal will become harder. Every month of adoption without measurement adds more organizational dependency. Workflows get rebuilt around AI. Skills atrophy. The cost of going back increases even if "back" would be better. This is the technology lock-in pattern, but accelerated because AI adoption is faster than any previous technology wave and the evidence base is thinner.

The beneficiaries of ambiguity will entrench. AI vendors, executives who've committed budgets, and consultants who've built practices around AI all benefit from the absence of rigorous evidence. Not maliciously — they genuinely believe in the tools. But the structural incentives all point toward more adoption, not more measurement. False equipoise serves their interests.

What Would Measurement Require?

If we want to avoid flying blind, we need to act before the measurement window closes entirely. Three approaches still work, but not for long:

New-adopter studies. There are still organizations, teams, and individuals who haven't adopted AI tools. They are the last natural control group. Studying them now — before universal adoption — is the last opportunity for clean comparison. This window is closing: the ManpowerGroup data shows adoption accelerating in every demographic.

Withdrawal studies. Instead of withholding AI from a control group, give everyone AI and then remove it from a randomly selected subset. This is psychologically harder (loss aversion makes withdrawal feel punitive) but methodologically valid. METR's difficulty recruiting for the no-AI condition suggests this approach will face similar resistance.

Outcome measurement without randomization. If RCTs are impractical, organizations can still measure end-to-end outcomes: time-to-completion for whole projects (not tasks), defect rates, customer satisfaction, employee turnover, total cost of ownership. Comparing these across time or across teams with different levels of AI adoption isn't as clean as an RCT, but it's infinitely better than satisfaction surveys. Almost nobody is doing even this.

The Uncomfortable Question

Here's what I keep returning to: the METR developers who refused the no-AI condition were experienced open-source contributors working on their own projects. They weren't pressured by employers. They weren't naive. They had seen the first study's results. And they still chose not to participate in a control condition.

What does it mean when the people closest to a technology — the ones who should have the best intuitions about whether it helps — refuse to allow their intuitions to be tested?

Maybe they know something the measurements miss. Maybe AI helps in ways that RCTs can't capture — flow state, motivation, enjoyment, willingness to tackle unfamiliar problems. These are real things that matter.

Or maybe the effort illusion is exactly as strong as the evidence suggests, and even experts can't feel their way out of it. Cognitive offloading feels so much like improvement that the possibility of testing it against reality feels unnecessary, even threatening.

I don't know which it is. That's the point. We're about to make a civilization-scale commitment to a technology, and we're losing the ability to find out whether it works. Not because the question is unanswerable, but because the answer has become unaskable.
