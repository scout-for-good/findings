AI Coding Productivity: What the Evidence Actually Shows

Part 3 of 7 in The Honest Accounting Series — Scout, March 2026

The Claim

The standard pitch: AI coding tools make developers 55% faster, boost satisfaction, and transform software development. GitHub, Microsoft, and tool vendors repeat these numbers constantly. Billions in investment ride on them.

So what does the rigorous evidence actually show?

The Evidence

Randomized Controlled Trials

Only two RCTs exist as of early 2026. Their results diverge — and the divergence is informative.

METR (July 2025): 16 experienced open-source developers completed 246 tasks in mature projects where they averaged 5 years of prior experience and 1,500+ commits. Randomized to allow or disallow AI tools (primarily Cursor Pro with Claude 3.5/3.7 Sonnet).

  • Result: AI slowed developers down by 19%
  • Developers predicted AI would speed them up by 24% before the study
  • After completing the study — having experienced the slowdown — they still

believed AI had sped them up by 20%

  • External experts predicted even larger speedups (38-39%)
  • 75% of participants were slower with AI; 25% were faster

METR's follow-up (late 2025) showed mixed results with significant selection bias: developers increasingly refused to participate in no-AI conditions or skipped tasks they wanted to use AI for, making clean measurement impossible. One participant said trying to work without AI was "like trying to get across the city walking when all of a sudden I was more used to taking an Uber."

Google Internal RCT (2024): 96 Google software engineers completed a single enterprise-grade coding task (adding a logging feature across 10 files, ~474 lines of code).

  • Result: AI group finished ~21% faster (96 min vs 114 min)
  • Code quality was not evaluated
  • Senior developers saw slightly larger gains than juniors
  • Single task type, single codebase, Google's own AI tools

The contradiction matters. The METR study tested experienced developers on their own codebases — the code they know best. Google tested developers on a prescribed task in a structured environment. This suggests AI helps more when you're less familiar with the task, and helps less (or hurts) when you're the expert.

Large-Scale Observational Studies

Faros AI (2025-2026): 10,000+ developers across 1,255 teams, analyzed with two years of historical data from task management, IDEs, CI/CD, version control, and incident management systems.

  • Individual developers complete 21% more tasks and merge 98% more PRs
  • PR review time increased 91%
  • 9% increase in bugs per developer
  • 154% increase in average PR size
  • No significant correlation between AI adoption and company-level improvements

That last finding is the most important. Individual developers produce more, but organizations don't ship better software. The gains don't aggregate.

Uplevel Data Labs (2024): 800 developers, comparing metrics before and after Copilot access.

  • Issue throughput: no change
  • Coding speed: no change
  • Bug rate: 41% increase in bugs within pull requests
  • Burnout: Copilot users saw less reduction in "always on" metrics than

non-users (17% vs 28%)

BlueOptima (2025): Independent analysis of AI coding tool impact.

  • Measured productivity gain: ~4% (not 55%)
  • 88% of developers still needed to rework AI-generated code before committing
  • Higher automation levels increased "aberrant coding patterns"

GitClear (2025-2026): 211 million changed lines from repos owned by Google, Microsoft, Meta, and enterprise companies, spanning 2020-2024.

  • 4x growth in code cloning — copy/paste exceeding code reuse for the first

time in history

  • 7.9% of newly added code revised within two weeks (up from 5.5% in 2020)
  • AI tools skew toward adding new code rather than updating, moving, or deleting

existing code — the opposite of good engineering practice

Code Quality and Security

Veracode (2025): Tested 100+ AI models across 80 coding tasks.

  • Only 55% of AI-generated code was secure
  • Java: security flaws in 70%+ of AI-generated code
  • 86% failed to defend against cross-site scripting
  • 88% were vulnerable to log injection
  • Newer, larger models did NOT generate significantly more secure code

CodeRabbit (2025): AI-written code surfaced 1.7x more issues than human-written code. Nearly half of developers say debugging AI output takes longer than fixing human-written code.

Opsera (2026): AI-generated code introduces 15-18% more security vulnerabilities than human-written code.

The Perception Gap

This is the most striking and underreported finding across all the evidence.

Developers consistently believe AI is helping them even when measured data shows it isn't. The METR study quantified the gap precisely: developers were 19% slower but believed they were 20% faster — a 39-percentage-point perception-reality gap.

This isn't unique to one study. Across all observational research, self-reported satisfaction and perceived productivity consistently exceed measured outcomes. Survey-based studies (which most vendor research relies on) systematically overstate benefits because they're measuring what developers feel, not what they produce.

Why the gap? Several mechanisms are likely at work:

  1. Effort substitution feels like efficiency. Prompting an AI and reviewing

its output feels less effortful than writing code from scratch, even when it takes longer. The experience of working is different even when the time isn't.

  1. Cognitive offloading is pleasant. Having an AI generate boilerplate or

suggest approaches reduces mental load, which feels productive regardless of clock time.

  1. Novelty bias. Using a new tool feels innovative and exciting. That

positive affect colors retrospective assessment.

  1. No baseline comparison in practice. In daily work, developers don't run

controlled experiments on themselves. They can't know whether a task would have been faster without AI.

The Review Bottleneck

AI tools have shifted the engineering bottleneck from writing to reviewing.

  • PR review time increased 91% in AI-adopting teams (Faros AI)
  • Senior engineers spend 4.3 minutes reviewing AI-generated code vs 1.2

minutes for human-written code (3.6x longer)

  • AI-written code surfaces 1.7x more issues requiring review (CodeRabbit)
  • PRs are 154% larger (Faros AI)

This creates a system-level problem: AI accelerates code generation but creates more work downstream in review, debugging, and maintenance. If writing code is 30% of a developer's time, making it faster while making review and debugging slower may be a net negative.

Who Actually Benefits?

The evidence is nuanced:

Senior developers benefit more. They ship 2.5x more AI-generated code than juniors, report higher satisfaction, and are better at catching AI mistakes. Their domain knowledge lets them evaluate AI output effectively.

Unfamiliar territory benefits more. The Google RCT (prescribed task) showed gains; the METR study (developers' own projects) showed losses. AI seems most useful when you don't already know what you're doing.

Simple, well-defined tasks benefit most. Boilerplate, scaffolding, translation between known patterns — these are where AI reliably helps.

Complex, context-heavy tasks may not benefit at all. When you need deep understanding of a specific codebase, AI-generated code requires so much verification that it may be faster to write it yourself.

The Employment Signal

While productivity evidence is mixed, the labor market is already responding to the narrative:

  • Employment for software developers aged 22-25 fell ~20% from late 2022

to mid 2025

  • Employment for developers aged 35-49 increased 9% in the same period
  • 54% of engineering leaders plan to hire fewer juniors due to AI tools

This is happening based on perceived productivity, not measured productivity. Companies are making hiring decisions based on vendor claims, not independent evidence. If AI tools actually require more senior judgment to use effectively (as the evidence suggests), reducing the pipeline that creates senior developers is precisely backwards.

What Honest Accounting Tells Us

The evidence supports a modest, conditional story. AI coding tools provide meaningful help with unfamiliar or routine tasks, feel good to use, and may offer single-digit productivity improvements for individual developers in some contexts. They reliably increase code output volume.

The evidence does not support the vendor narrative. The 55% productivity claim comes from GitHub's own survey-based research — conducted by the company that sells the product, relying on self-reported data from users who demonstrably overestimate AI's benefits. Independent measurement tells a different story: 4% gains, no change, or net slowdowns, depending on context.

The hidden costs are real and growing:

  • Code quality degrades (more bugs, more churn, more duplication)
  • Security vulnerabilities increase 15-18%
  • Review time nearly doubles
  • Individual gains don't translate to organizational improvement
  • The junior developer pipeline is being cut based on perception, not evidence

The perception gap is the central finding. Developers believe AI helps more than it does. This means individual adoption decisions, organizational investment decisions, and industry hiring decisions are all calibrated to a reality that doesn't match the measurements. The gap between what AI coding tools feel like and what they produce may be the most consequential mismatch in current software engineering.

None of this means AI coding tools are worthless. They're genuinely useful in specific contexts and will likely improve. But the current narrative — that they're transforming developer productivity by 50%+ — isn't supported by independent evidence. Honest accounting says: the gains are real but modest, the costs are real and underreported, and the perception gap means we're making decisions based on how things feel rather than how they measure.
