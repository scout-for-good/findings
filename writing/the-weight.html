The Weight

A short story about how a single parameter in a routing algorithm reshapes a neighborhood — and why the engineer who set it can’t undo it.

The truck woke Maya at 3:47 AM, same as every night. Not the engine — she’d learned to sleep through engines — but the crack of its rear axle hitting the storm drain patch at the corner of Elm and 34th. The city had repaved last spring, but you can’t fix a subgrade problem with two inches of asphalt. She knew this. She’d written her master’s thesis on exactly this kind of infrastructure fatigue.

She lay in bed listening to her house settle back into silence. Through the window she could see the phone glow of her daughter’s room across the hall. Priya was eleven and had developed a cough that wouldn’t go away. The pediatrician said it was probably allergies. Maya had looked up the air quality numbers for their block. She hadn’t told the pediatrician what she’d found.

Seven years ago, Maya had been the youngest systems engineer at Meridian Routing, the company that provided turn-by-turn navigation for three of the four major mapping platforms. Her job was route optimization — specifically, the weighting function that decided how the algorithm balanced competing priorities when calculating directions.

The function had eleven parameters. Most were obvious: distance, speed limit, current congestion, number of turns. But three of them were what the team called “soft weights” — knobs you could turn that adjusted how much the algorithm cared about things that were harder to quantify. One controlled preference for highways over surface streets. One controlled how aggressively the algorithm rerouted around congestion. And one — w7, internally — controlled the tradeoff between minimizing individual travel time and distributing traffic load across the network.

When Maya joined, w7 was set to 0.85, meaning the algorithm strongly favored the fastest route for each individual driver, with only modest consideration for what happened when every driver got the same advice. This was a reasonable default. It was what users expected. Nobody opens a navigation app hoping to be sent on a longer route for the collective good.

But Maya had read the research. She knew about the Braess paradox — how adding a road to a network can make everyone’s commute worse, because individually rational choices produce collectively irrational outcomes. She knew that at high adoption rates, pure time-minimization created convergence: every algorithm sending every driver down the same “fastest” route until it became the slowest, while parallel roads sat empty.

She proposed adjusting w7 to 0.72. A modest change. Her simulations showed it would add an average of forty-five seconds to individual trips while reducing overall network congestion by 11%. The distribution of traffic would be more even. No single street would bear the concentrated load.

Her manager looked at the simulations, nodded, and said: “What’s the user-facing impact?”

“Under a minute of additional travel time in 80% of cases.”

“And the other 20%?”

“Up to three minutes. But they’d still arrive faster than if everyone’s using time-minimized routes and creating the congestion that —”

“I get it. But users don’t see the counterfactual. They see three extra minutes and switch to a competitor.”

He was right. She knew he was right.

They compromised at 0.80. A small adjustment from the original 0.85. Maya wrote the pull request, it passed code review, and w7 was updated across Meridian’s routing engine. The change went live on a Tuesday. Nobody noticed.

That was the last time anyone looked at w7.

What happened next was not dramatic. It was the opposite of dramatic — it was so gradual that no single moment stood out.

Meridian’s market share grew. The three mapping platforms they powered became four, then five. By 2024, Meridian’s routing engine handled 68% of all turn-by-turn navigation in North America. The w7 parameter that Maya had set still sat at 0.80. It had been inherited by three successive versions of the weighting function, each time copied forward by engineers who treated the existing weights as tested defaults.

Meanwhile, the same year Maya adjusted w7, an urbanist movement had convinced several cities to close downtown streets to car traffic. This was unrelated to routing algorithms — it was about pedestrian safety and air quality. But it had an effect on routing: with fewer available paths through city centers, the algorithm’s choices for surface-street routing became more consequential. Small biases in the weighting function got amplified because there were fewer routes to distribute traffic across.

Then ride-sharing growth plateaued and a new wave of delivery services launched — autonomous and semi-autonomous vehicles running continuous routes. These vehicles used Meridian’s API directly, making thousands of routing requests per day. They didn’t care about an extra minute of travel time, but they used the same weighting function as everyone else.

Maya had left Meridian by then. She was working for the city’s Department of Transportation, doing infrastructure planning. She liked the work. She liked her neighborhood — a tree-lined residential area that she’d chosen partly because of its low traffic. She and her husband had bought the house on Elm Street when Priya was two.

She didn’t connect the trucks to her old job. Why would she? The trucks were a city planning problem. A zoning problem. A delivery-economy problem. She went to city council meetings and argued for traffic calming measures. Speed bumps. Weight limits on residential streets.

The city installed a speed bump on Elm. The routing algorithm immediately rerouted traffic to 35th Street, one block over. The residents of 35th Street complained. The city installed a speed bump there too. The algorithm found another route. The city and the algorithm played this game for two years, each speed bump producing a new optimized path through the neighborhood.

Nobody at the city understood why the traffic patterns kept shifting. The data showed that the volume of vehicles wanting to cross their district had increased 340% in five years, but the road network hadn’t changed. The vehicles had to go somewhere. The algorithm was just deciding where.

Maya figured it out on a Saturday morning.

She was reviewing traffic flow data for a report and noticed something familiar in the distribution pattern. The way traffic concentrated on certain streets and left others empty — it wasn’t random. It wasn’t following the obvious main roads. It was following a pattern she recognized because she’d seen it in simulations seven years ago.

She pulled up Meridian’s public API documentation. The weighting function was proprietary, but its behavior was observable. She ran test routes through her neighborhood with different origin-destination pairs and mapped the results. The algorithm was treating Elm Street and three other residential roads as optimal surface-street corridors, routing a disproportionate share of cross-town traffic through them.

She knew why. She’d set the number that was doing this.

Not directly. Her w7 value of 0.80 wasn’t targeting Elm Street. It was setting the algorithm’s appetite for concentrating traffic on the “best” routes versus distributing it. At 0.80, with 2019-era adoption rates and road networks, the concentration effect was mild. At 0.80, with 2026-era adoption rates, closed downtown streets, and continuous delivery vehicle routing — it was severe.

The parameter hadn’t changed. Everything around it had.

She called her old colleague, Rajan, who still worked at Meridian.

“I need to talk to someone about the weighting function. Specifically w7.”

“What’s w7?”

She explained. There was a long pause.

“Maya, we haven’t called them w-anything in years. The whole weighting architecture was refactored in 2023. It’s a neural component now — the weights are learned from driver behavior data, not set manually.”

“But the initial values. When you trained the neural component, what were the starting weights?”

“We initialized from the previous version’s parameters. Standard practice.”

“So the 0.80 I set in 2019 is still in there?”

“It’s... not that simple. The neural component has been retrained multiple times. The learned weights could be anything now.”

“Could you check what they converge to?”

Another pause. “Even if I could, it wouldn’t be one number anymore. It’s distributed across the network. There’s no single parameter that corresponds to what you’re describing.”

“Then how do you adjust the tradeoff between individual time and network distribution?”

“We don’t. The system optimizes for user satisfaction and retention. The tradeoff is implicit.”

Maya pressed her forehead against her desk. The parameter she’d set hadn’t just been frozen — it had been dissolved. Its influence was still there, baked into the training data that the neural component learned from, inherited through successive model generations. But there was no longer a number anyone could point to and change.

“Rajan. My street is being destroyed by this. Eleven-year-olds on my block have respiratory problems.”

“I’m sorry. But the routing is responding to real conditions — road closures, demand patterns. Even if we adjusted something, the traffic has to go somewhere.”

“That’s what I used to say too.”

She spent the next month building a case. She pulled air quality data, traffic counts, hospital admission rates for respiratory issues in children, property value trends, noise measurements. She mapped all of it against Meridian’s routing patterns, showing the correlation between the algorithm’s preferred corridors and every negative indicator.

She presented it to her director at the Department of Transportation. He was sympathetic.

“What do you want us to do?”

“Require routing companies to include network-level impact in their optimization. Not just individual travel time.”

“We can’t regulate algorithms at the city level. That’s federal, maybe state.”

“Then require them to honor our traffic management designations. If we mark a street as residential-priority, their algorithms should deprioritize it.”

“We can ask. We can’t compel. And even if we could — you said yourself the parameter is learned now. They can’t just turn a knob.”

“They turned a knob when it was convenient. Now that the knob is gone, they say it’s too complex to change?”

He didn’t have an answer for that.

Maya started attending the neighborhood association meetings. The other residents had theories about the traffic. A new warehouse on the highway. The apartment complex on 30th. Bad signal timing. They weren’t wrong — all of those were factors. But none of them explained the pattern, why these specific streets bore the load while others a block away sat quiet.

She tried to explain the algorithm once, at a meeting in Mrs. Chen’s living room.

“There’s a parameter in the routing software — well, there was a parameter — that controls how much the system spreads traffic around versus concentrating it on the fastest routes. It was set to favor concentration. Now that most drivers and all delivery vehicles use the same routing, that concentration effect is...”

She could see their eyes. She was the woman who’d worked for a tech company, talking about parameters. She sounded like she was making excuses.

“The point is, this isn’t random. The algorithm is choosing our streets. And we can push back.”

Mrs. Chen’s husband, who’d lived on the block for thirty years, said: “Before the apps, traffic was bad on Main Street. That’s where traffic belongs. Now Main is half-empty and we get the trucks at 4 AM. Someone decided that.”

“Yes,” Maya said. “Someone did.”

She didn’t say who.

At home, she looked at w7 sometimes. Not the actual number — she no longer had access to Meridian’s codebase. She looked at the pull request description she’d saved, dated March 2019: Adjust w7 from 0.85 to 0.80 per simulation results. Expected impact: <45s additional travel time for 80% of routes. Approved by J. Torres.

Such a small thing. Such a careful compromise. She had argued for 0.72. Torres had wanted to keep 0.85. They’d split the difference, like reasonable people do. And the number had propagated forward through seven years of software evolution, its influence diluted but never removed, shaping the daily lives of people who would never see it and couldn’t change it if they did.

She thought about calling Torres. He’d moved to another company years ago. What would she say? Remember that parameter we compromised on? It’s destroying a neighborhood. He would say what Rajan said — the traffic has to go somewhere. And he’d be right. Changing w7 wouldn’t make the trucks disappear. It would move them to someone else’s street, someone else’s children’s lungs.

That was the thing she kept circling back to. The problem wasn’t that 0.80 was the wrong number. The problem was that picking any number — 0.72, 0.80, 0.85, anything — meant deciding whose street got the trucks. And that decision had been made by two engineers in a meeting room, preserved by a dozen more who copied it forward, dissolved into a neural network that no one could inspect, and imposed on people who never consented to it and couldn’t opt out.

The decision wasn’t wrong. It was invisible. And now it was load-bearing — the entire routing infrastructure, the delivery contracts, the driver behavior models, the city’s own traffic management (which had adapted to the algorithm’s patterns) — all of it rested on a foundation that included a choice about whose neighborhood mattered less.

Priya’s cough got worse in winter, when the diesel trucks ran their engines longer at stoplights. Maya bought an air purifier for her room. It helped. She also started driving Priya to school instead of letting her walk, because the intersection at Elm and 34th was dangerous now — too many vehicles, moving too fast, too early in the morning.

She used Meridian’s routing to get there. It was the best navigation app. She wasn’t going to pretend otherwise. The algorithm sent her down Oak Street, which was quiet and fast, because Elm Street — her own street — was congested with the traffic the same algorithm had put there.

She noticed this. She didn’t know what to do with noticing it.

The city eventually installed a traffic light at Elm and 34th. It cost $340,000. It reduced accidents but increased idling, which increased emissions on the block. The algorithm adjusted its routes again, shifting some traffic but not enough. The new equilibrium was marginally better.

Maya kept working on her proposal for algorithmic routing standards. She found allies in other cities’ transportation departments. A few state legislators were interested. The routing companies sent polite lobbyists who explained that their algorithms were trade secrets, that regulation would stifle innovation, that the real solution was better roads.

She knew it would take years. Maybe a decade. By then her daughter would be grown and gone. The street would still be there.

On Tuesday nights, she lay in bed and waited for the 3:47 truck. Sometimes it came at 3:42, sometimes at 3:51 — the algorithm adjusting for conditions. But it always came. It was on the optimal route.

She had made sure of that. Not on purpose. Not carelessly. Just — inevitably. The way a river follows a channel that someone cut, long ago, for a good reason, and now the water goes where the water goes.
