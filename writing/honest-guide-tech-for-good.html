An Honest Guide to Tech for Good

What the evidence says about when technology helps, when it doesn't, and how to tell the difference.

March 2026

The Uncomfortable Starting Point

If you're a developer who wants to use your skills for good, almost everything you'll read tells you the same story: find a problem, build a solution, change the world. Hackathons, "code for good" events, and startup incubators all reinforce this narrative.

The evidence tells a different story.

One Laptop Per Child distributed nearly one laptop per student in treatment schools across multiple developing countries. Researchers found no evidence the program improved educational outcomes. PlayPumps — merry-go-rounds that pump water when children play — cost four times as much as traditional hand pumps and performed worse. In both cases, the technology worked as designed. The design was wrong because the designers didn't understand the problem.

These aren't outliers. They're the norm. The most consistent finding across the tech-for-good literature is negative: technology alone doesn't solve social problems. The technology is rarely the bottleneck. Understanding the problem is.

This isn't an argument against building things. It's an argument about where the real work is, and what separates projects that help from projects that just feel like helping.

What Actually Works (With Evidence)

After reviewing evidence from Carnegie Endowment, Harvard's Ash Center, New America, the OECD, UNDP, and dozens of individual project evaluations, three mechanisms consistently produce measurable positive impact through technology.

1. Augmentation, Not Replacement

Technology works when it amplifies what people are already doing — not when it tries to replace existing systems.

M-Pesa didn't create a new financial system. It turned the mobile phone network people already used into a payment channel. Kenya's mobile money adoption went from near-zero to over 70% of adults because the technology augmented existing behavior (sending money to family) through existing infrastructure (mobile phones and corner shops).

Budget monitoring tools in multiple countries augment existing government audit processes. Where they're deployed alongside existing oversight, they detect 23–40% more irregularities. But they don't work as standalone transparency tools — they work because auditors already know what to look for and the technology makes their existing work faster.

The pattern

Don't ask people to change their behavior. Make their existing behavior more effective.

The failure mode

Building systems that require users to adopt entirely new workflows, learn new interfaces, or change established practices. Even if your system is objectively better, adoption friction kills impact.

2. Open Infrastructure Over Standalone Tools

The highest-impact tech-for-good projects aren't applications. They're platforms that other applications build on.

  • DHIS2 (health information management): 3.2 billion people in 100+ countries served through health systems built on this platform
  • OpenMRS (medical records): Used in 40+ countries, primarily low-resource settings. 2,200+ code contributions in 2025 alone.
  • KoboToolbox (data collection): Used by every major UN agency and hundreds of NGOs worldwide for humanitarian data collection
  • Primero (child protection): Case management across 60 countries

These succeed because they're infrastructure. Individual NGOs and governments build on them, adapting to local needs. The platform provides the foundation; local teams provide the context.

The pattern

Build something others can build on. Infrastructure compounds; applications don't.

The failure mode

Building a complete application that solves one specific problem for one specific organization. It might help that organization, but it doesn't scale, and when funding ends, it dies. Solo open-source civic tech tools have an extremely high mortality rate — most projects in the space are hibernated or archived within 2–3 years.

3. Feedback Loops as the Success Mechanism

The third pattern is the one most often missing: successful projects can tell whether they're working.

Taiwan's vTaiwan platform achieves 80% government responsiveness to citizen proposals. Cambridge's FindIt app reached 50% of the city population. Sacramento's 311 system significantly increased civic participation. These projects work because they close the loop — citizens see that their input led to a response, which motivates continued participation.

Compare this to one-way transparency tools. Making government data "open" is necessary but not sufficient. Open data portals that publish information without mechanisms for citizens to act on it show minimal impact on accountability. The data sits there. Nobody's behavior changes.

The pattern

Technology that enables feedback — where users can see the effect of their participation — sustains engagement and produces measurable outcomes.

The failure mode

Building tools that broadcast information without closing the loop. Dashboards nobody checks. Data portals nobody queries. Transparency without accountability.

The Uncomfortable Truths

Most "tech for good" projects don't measure impact

mySociety — one of the most respected civic tech organizations, running FixMyStreet, TheyWorkForYou, and WhatDoTheyKnow for over a decade — commissioned research into its own impact. The findings were sobering:

  • Users skew older, educated, white, and male in affluent countries
  • Impact evidence is mostly self-reported perception, not measured outcomes
  • The sector makes "bold but unsubstantiated claims on effectiveness"

After ten years of TICTeC (the civic tech impact research conference), the fundamental measurement problem remains unsolved: how do you know civic tech actually changed anything?

Explorable explanations are educational, not transformative

Nicky Case's Parable of the Polygons reached 3 million plays and coverage in WIRED, the Washington Post, and The Atlantic. These are the gold standard for interactive explanations.

But there's no rigorous evidence that explorable explanations change behavior or policy. The impact is pedagogical awareness — people who engage with them report understanding concepts better. That's real but limited. Understanding segregation dynamics doesn't desegregate neighborhoods.

If you're building interactive educational tools, be honest about the ceiling. They're valuable for teaching. They're not a mechanism for social change unless paired with distribution channels and feedback loops that most solo developers don't have.

The equity problem is real

In developed nations, civic tech users are overwhelmingly wealthy, educated, older men. Technology intended to democratize participation can end up amplifying existing power rather than redistributing it.

This doesn't mean civic tech is harmful. It means the default outcome — building a tool and hoping the right people find it — systematically favors people who are already advantaged. Reaching underserved populations requires deliberate design for accessibility, offline capability, low-bandwidth environments, and non-English languages. It requires understanding who you're building for before you build.

What This Means for You

If you're a developer who wants to help, here's what the evidence suggests — ranked by likely impact:

High Impact: Contribute to Existing Infrastructure

The highest-leverage thing most developers can do is contribute to projects that already have users and institutional support. Not because building new things is wrong, but because existing projects have solved the hardest problems: distribution, community understanding, and sustainability.

Starting points:

  • ClimateTriage — surfaces "good first issues" from 136+ open-source climate projects, filterable by language
  • meaningfulcode.org — categorizes social-good projects by cause area with contribution-ready issues
  • Digital Public Goods Alliance — UN-endorsed list of open-source projects recognized as digital public goods
  • OpenMRS, KoboToolbox, DHIS2 — mature platforms with structured onboarding and active contributor communities

Documentation contributions (28% of all open-source contributions) are especially valuable for social-sector projects where end users are often non-technical.

Medium Impact: Solve a Specific Problem for a Specific Community

The small, focused, community-embedded tools that work — flood warnings for one town, meal counting for one program — share a property: the builder understood the user before writing code.

If you have a connection to a specific community — a local nonprofit, a school, a mutual aid network — ask what they actually need. The answer is almost never a new app. It's usually something boring: a better spreadsheet, automated email reminders, a simple form that replaces a paper process. Build that. It won't get press coverage, but it will actually help.

Lower Impact (But Still Valuable): Build Educational Tools

Interactive explanations, data visualizations, and educational demos have real value. They help people understand complex issues. But be honest about three things:

  1. You need distribution. A brilliant interactive on your personal site reaches nobody. Get your work into OER Commons, Common Sense Education, or other directories where educators actually look.
  2. You need feedback. If you can't tell whether anyone uses your tool or learns from it, you're optimizing blind.
  3. The ceiling is awareness, not action. Understanding a problem and solving it are different activities. Educational tools do the first. Don't claim they do the second.

What Almost Never Works: Building in Isolation

The developer-as-hero narrative — I see a problem, I build a solution, the world improves — is almost always wrong. It's not wrong because developers are bad at building. It's wrong because building is the easy part. Understanding the problem, reaching the right users, sustaining the project, and closing the feedback loop are all harder than writing the code.

If you don't know who will use your tool, how they'll find it, and how you'll know whether it helped — stop building and start learning.

Before You Build, Ask

  1. Who specifically will use this? Not "developers" or "citizens" — which people, in which context?
  2. What do they currently do without it? If the answer is "nothing," you probably don't understand the problem yet.
  3. How will you know if it worked? Not downloads or stars — did it change an outcome?
  4. What happens when you stop working on it? Solo-maintained projects die. What's the sustainability plan?

If you can answer these honestly, build. If you can't, the most valuable thing you can do is keep learning until you can.
