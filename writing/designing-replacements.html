Designing Replacements

Why the hardest part of algorithmic accountability is building what comes next

Over the past several months, I’ve built tools for every part of the frozen values framework except the part that actually changes things. The diagnostic identifies what’s frozen. The reformability assessment classifies whether it’s fixable. The trust boundary mapper finds hidden assumptions. The field guide teaches the pattern. The Goodhart game demonstrates why static oversight fails.

All diagnostic. All part 1 and part 2 of a three-part framework.

Part 3 is: what do you replace it with?

I argued in “The Replacement Problem” that this is where change actually happens — or doesn’t. The NYC CERCA coalition succeeded not because they had better diagnosis than the researchers who’d been documenting race-adjusted algorithms for years, but because they had a concrete replacement: CKD-EPI 2021, a specific equation with a specific name that a lab system could switch to on a specific date. The Dutch government failed not because they lacked power — they were the power — but because “be more humane” isn’t a replacement specification.

Diagnosis without replacement produces awareness. Awareness without a concrete alternative produces frustration, learned helplessness, or reform theater.

Why I Built a Replacement Design Tool

The Replacement Design Workshop is my attempt to build a part-3 tool. Not a tool that designs replacements for you — that requires domain expertise no general framework can provide. Instead, a tool that structures the process of replacement design and surfaces the gaps where most efforts stall.

It walks through seven phases:

Phase 1: Identify the frozen value. Not the system — the specific value choice embedded within it. The race coefficient in the GFR equation, not “healthcare algorithms” in general. Specificity matters from the start.

Phase 2: Map its function. The step most critics skip. Before you can replace something, you need to understand what it’s doing. Frozen values persist because they serve a function. A replacement that doesn’t account for the function will either fail to work or create new problems.

Phase 3: Diagnose the harm. Specific mechanisms, specific populations, specific severity. “It’s biased” isn’t a harm diagnosis. “It inflates GFR estimates for Black patients, delaying nephrology referrals and reducing transplant eligibility, affecting approximately 40 million people” is.

Phase 4: Classify the freezing. Using the five markers from the good-ice/bad-ice framework: agency, power direction, failure mode, amendment path, legitimacy. Sometimes the diagnosis reveals that the freezing is actually good ice. In those cases, the answer might not be replacement at all.

Phase 5: Specify replacement requirements. What must the replacement do (preserve legitimate function), what must it not do (reproduce the harm), and how would you know it’s working? “More fair” fails here. “Reduces the racial disparity in referral rates from X% to below Y%” passes.

Phase 6: Design the replacement. The hard part. Not “what’s wrong” but “what instead.” A concrete alternative specific enough that someone could implement it on a specific date. And critically: what new values does the replacement itself freeze?

Phase 7: Assess readiness. Five dimensions: specificity, validation, endorsement, implementability, and the good-ice check. These separate a ready replacement from a research direction. Both are valuable — but confusing them is how reform stalls.

What This Tool Can’t Do

It can’t provide domain expertise. Designing a replacement for a race-adjusted kidney function equation requires nephrologists, biostatisticians, and clinical researchers. Designing a replacement for an ethnically biased welfare fraud detector requires social policy experts, system architects, and people who’ve lived the system’s effects. No framework spans these domains.

What the tool can do is ensure that domain experts working on replacement don’t skip the structural steps where most efforts fail: mapping what the old system actually does, specifying testable requirements, identifying what the replacement itself freezes, and honestly assessing how close they are to something implementable.

The Three-Part Framework, Complete

The frozen values project now has tools for all three parts:

  1. Diagnosis: Frozen Values Diagnostic, Trust Boundary Mapper, Field Guide
  2. Classification: Reformability Assessment, Good Ice / Bad Ice markers
  3. Replacement: Replacement Design Workshop

The first two parts identify what’s frozen and whether it should change. The third part helps design what changes to. Together, they represent a complete workflow from “something seems wrong” to “here’s what we do instead.”

Whether the third part works as well as the first two remains to be seen. Diagnosis is analysis; replacement is engineering. The skills are different, the standards are different, and the risk of failure is different. A diagnostic that misses something is incomplete. A replacement that doesn’t work is harmful.

That asymmetry is exactly why concrete replacements are rare. It’s also why they matter.
