Unfreezing from Below

When users push back against design decisions they never agreed to

The Missing Half

The freezing framework describes how values get encoded into systems: someone makes a choice, it becomes a parameter, infrastructure builds on it, and eventually no one remembers it was a choice at all. The framework tracks a lifecycle — freeze, function, breakdown, visibility, repair, re-freeze — but until now, it's been told entirely from the builder's perspective. Designers freeze. Systems function. Breakdowns happen passively. Repair is institutional.

This misses what actually happens. Users don't wait for systems to break. They push back. They route around. They modify, appropriate, hack, and refuse. There is a whole taxonomy of user responses to frozen design decisions, and each one carries a signal about which freezes are wrong.

A Taxonomy of Unfreezing

Users challenge frozen design decisions in at least five distinct ways, escalating in effort and confrontation:

1. Desire Paths

The gentlest form. Users simply go where the design didn't intend. In urban planning, these are literal dirt tracks worn through grass — shortcuts the designed walkways didn't provide. In software, they're workarounds: the spreadsheet maintained alongside the official system, the copy-paste workflow that bypasses the intended process, the text message sent instead of the official channel.

Desire paths are diagnostic. They tell you which frozen decisions don't match real needs. Finnish city officials document where people walk after the first snowfall, then use that data to redesign trail systems. Some universities — Virginia Tech, UC Berkeley — wait to see where students actually walk before paving pathways.

The signal: "Your design doesn't match how we actually work."

2. Appropriation

Users repurpose the system for something the designer never intended. SMS was designed for network engineers to send test messages — users turned it into the dominant communication medium of a generation. Twitter's hashtag was invented by users, not the platform. Skateboarders treat every urban surface as potential terrain, reading the city against its intended grain.

Appropriation is creative, not destructive. It discovers latent possibilities in a design. When McDonald's launched #McDStories expecting warm memories and got food poisoning reports instead, users weren't breaking the hashtag — they were using it exactly as designed, just not as intended. The design froze a mechanism (public aggregation) while assuming it could control the content. Users proved otherwise.

The signal: "This system is more useful than you thought, in ways you didn't plan."

3. Modification

Users actively change the system. Jailbreaking phones, modding games, hardware hacking, custom firmware. This goes beyond routing around — it intervenes in the frozen decision itself.

The modding community is massive and economically significant. Minecraft mods created entire genres. Hardware modders extend product lifetimes by years. The jailbreaking community pioneered features (multitasking, custom keyboards, third-party app stores) that manufacturers eventually adopted officially.

The signal: "The freeze is wrong, and we can prove it by building the alternative."

4. Repair

Users maintain, restore, or fix systems the manufacturer tried to make disposable or service-dependent. The right-to-repair movement — now with legislation in all 50 US states — is explicitly about unfreezing design decisions around repairability. When John Deere locked tractor firmware so only authorized dealers could diagnose electronic issues, farmers didn't accept the freeze. They fought it in legislatures, in courts, and with Ukrainian-sourced bootleg firmware.

The 2026 legislative template is revealing: it expands "tools" to include "software, data files, activation mechanisms, and security credentials required to complete a repair." Every one of those is a frozen design decision that someone tried to make permanent.

The signal: "We own this. Your freeze on our autonomy is illegitimate."

5. Resistance

The strongest response: users refuse the system entirely. In enterprise IT, this shows up as "shadow IT" — departments running their own tools because the official systems don't serve them. In urban design, it's desire paths that become so established they erode the designed landscape. In product design, it's the return, the bad review, the switch to a competitor.

Resistance is the most expensive signal for everyone involved. Users lose the system's benefits. Builders lose users. But it's also the clearest signal: this freeze is unacceptable.

The signal: "We'd rather have nothing than what you froze."

The Designer's Counter-Moves

When users unfreeze from below, designers respond in two fundamentally different ways:

Suppression

  • Hostile architecture: Camden benches designed to prevent sleeping, skateboarding, and sticker-placing. Anti-homeless spikes. Sloped window sills. The built environment as frozen hostility.
  • DRM and parts pairing: Technical locks that prevent modification or repair. Software that bricks devices after unauthorized service.
  • Legal barriers: DMCA section 1201, void-warranty threats, terms of service that criminalize appropriation.
  • Forced updates: Removing features users relied on, changing interfaces to prevent workarounds.

Suppression treats user unfreezing as a threat. It re-freezes harder. This works in the short term and fails in the long term — it drives users toward resistance (the most destructive response) and creates political movements (right to repair, anti-hostile-architecture campaigns, open source).

Accommodation

  • Design for appropriation: Deliberately leaving room for users to complete the design. Providing APIs, configuration options, plugin architectures. Alan Dix's framework: allow interpretation, expose intentions, support but don't control.
  • Observing desire paths: Using workaround patterns as design input. The Finnish snow-path method. A/B testing. Usage analytics that ask "what are users actually doing?" rather than "are users doing what we intended?"
  • Modular architecture: Isolating frozen decisions from fluid ones so users can modify what they need without breaking what works.
  • Right to repair compliance: Providing parts, tools, documentation, and diagnostic access.

Accommodation treats user unfreezing as governance feedback. It asks: which of our freezes are wrong?

The Engineering Connection

This maps precisely to how mature engineering organizations handle field feedback.

In product engineering, a warranty claim isn't just a cost — it's a signal that a design decision doesn't survive contact with real use. Field modifications tell you which tolerances were wrong, which material choices don't hold up, which assembly sequences create problems. Good engineering organizations have formal channels (Engineering Change Requests, Corrective Action Reports, Voice of Customer programs) that route these signals back to design teams.

Bad engineering organizations treat field modifications as warranty-voiding offenses and warranty claims as cost problems. They suppress the signal. Sound familiar?

The parallel extends further. In engineering, there's a recognized distinction between design intent and use case — and the gap between them is where products fail or get appropriated. Bill of Materials tracks what was designed. Field service records track what actually happens. The gap between them is the desire path, written in warranty data.

The Governance Insight

Here's what this adds to the freezing framework: user unfreezing is a governance mechanism.

The original framework identified breakdown as the moment when frozen values become visible again — Heidegger's present-at-hand, the wrench that only becomes visible when it breaks. But breakdown is passive. It waits for failure.

User unfreezing is active governance. It's people saying "this freeze is wrong" before the system breaks — sometimes before the system is even fully deployed. Desire paths appear immediately. Workarounds emerge within weeks. Mods and jailbreaks follow within months. The signal is there long before institutional breakdown.

The question isn't whether users will unfreeze your design decisions. They will. The question is whether your system has mechanisms to receive and act on that signal. Systems that suppress it get hostile architecture — technically sophisticated but morally bankrupt. Systems that accommodate it get desire paths paved into real walkways — designs that learn from the people who live with them.

A well-governed system doesn't just freeze well. It unfreezes well.

Implications

Three practical takeaways:

  1. Workarounds are diagnostic, not deviant. Every spreadsheet maintained alongside an official system, every jailbroken phone, every desire path in the grass is a user telling you which freeze is wrong. Treating these as violations (to be prevented) rather than signals (to be heard) is a governance failure.
  1. The right to unfreeze is a design principle. Design for appropriation, modular architecture, right to repair, open APIs — these aren't just nice features. They're the mechanism by which frozen design decisions get corrected. Systems without unfreezing mechanisms accumulate bad freezes until they fail catastrophically.
  1. Suppression escalates. When you suppress gentle unfreezing (desire paths, workarounds), you drive users toward more confrontational forms (modification, resistance, political movements). The cheapest freeze to fix is the one a desire path tells you about. The most expensive is the one that becomes a Supreme Court case.

The freezing framework started by asking: how do values become invisible infrastructure? This extension asks: what do the people living with that infrastructure do about it? The answer is consistent, cross-domain, and clear. They unfreeze. The only question is whether anyone is listening.
